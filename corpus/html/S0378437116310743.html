1. Introduction

Semantic networks have been used to characterize knowledge representation systems  [1], [2], [3]. Various methods have been developed for this purpose, almost always seeking comparisons between the topological indices of networks and the human properties of the system. One of the properties that remains poorly accessible to these methods is the level of disciplinarity of a discourse (i.e., from disciplinary to multidisciplinary)  [3]. The robustness properties of semantic networks can help in the development of more precise methods for the characterization of these systems. In this context, efforts have been undertaken to contribute to the understanding of semantic networks as a knowledge representation system that stems from its properties.

The behavior of semantic network-based systems can be analyzed with studies on the network’s topological structure. However, the study may be complemented by investigating a variety of processes that occur in networks. Therefore, this work focuses on the study of robustness of semantic networks of titles based on cliques (SNT) and evaluates the topological changes in the SNT structures when they are subjected to different vertex removal strategies.

Albert, Jeong and Barabási  [4] showed that scale-free networks (e.g., the WWW, Internet) are highly resilient to random faults, unlike what occurs in random networks whose network is highly fragmented after random and uniform removal of over 28% of the vertices. When networks are subjected to the removal of vertices that play a vital role in the network’s connectivity, authors  [4] showed that random networks and scale-free networks feature low robustness.

Crucitti et al.  [5] analyzed the overall efficiency of random and scale-free networks when subjected to the strategy of removing vertices according to their importance: degree, betweenness centrality and recalculated betweenness centrality each time a vertex is removed. They concluded that random networks, due to their homogeneity, exhibit similar tolerance to errors and attacks, whereas scale-free networks, due to their heterogeneity, are much more robust to errors and highly vulnerable to attacks.

Despite the presence of several studies on robustness and percolation in networks, there remains a gap in the study of the robustness of semantic networks of titles based on cliques, once this type of network is not a scale-free network according to Clauset et al.  [6], nor a random network. The objective of this article was to present a study on the resilience of semantic networks based on cliques when subjected to three vertex removal strategies. We analyzed the robustness of 15 semantic networks based on the titles of scientific journals built by Pereira et al.  [3].

The analysis of SNT robustness can promote the dissemination of knowledge because obtaining the SNTs that preserve topological characteristics after the removal of vertices decreases the data instance size. Thus, information can be searched on a smaller database. SNTs resulting from vertex removal can more efficiently assist researchers in their search for topics related to their research field.

2. Related work

2.1. Semantic networks

We propose the following definition of a semantic network: a semantic network is a system for representing knowledge imbued with an intention of functionality and established by context. A semantic network is composed of words, concepts or entities with semantic meaning and their relationships. We use graphs to represent it.

Steyvers and Tenenbaum  [7] do not endorse the traditional models based on Quillian  [8] and define a semantic network as an abstract representation of just one aspect of semantic knowledge—the relationships between pairs of words. For Steyvers and Tenenbaum  [7], relationships between words can be represented by edges or arcs in a semantic network. The semantic network model proposed by Steyvers and Tenenbaum  [7] also has limitations because it does not capture other aspects of a semantic structure, such as a sensitive context of meanings, the existence of different types of semantic relations or the precise nature of the relationship between words, meanings and concepts.

Cancho and Solé  [9] built semantic networks from sentences obtained from the British National Corpus. Each word of the collected sentence is a vertex, and edges are established if the word occurs in the same sentence. From the English Thesaurus, Motter et al.  [10] built a semantic network so that two words are connected if both express similar concepts. Only words that appear in the thesaurus entry were considered.

2.2. Semantic networks of cliques

Caldeira et al.  [1] and Teixeira et al.  [2] constructed and analyzed semantic networks of cliques from discourses. Caldeira et al.  [1] investigated 312 literary texts obtained mostly from Project Gutenberg, and Teixeira et al.  [2] investigated 12 oral discourses. Each word with intrinsic meaning and in its canonical form is a vertex, and two words are connected if they are in the same sentence.

Pereira et al.  [3] analyzed article titles from 15 scientific journals. A pre-treatment of the article titles was executed, which consisted of applying a series of rules, such as proper nouns must form a single word and sequences of words that have a proper meaning must form a single word. These rules are specified in Pereira et al.  [3]. Next, they applied the computational treatment developed by Caldeira et al.  [1], which consisted of eliminating words without intrinsic meaning (e.g., articles, pronouns, prepositions) from each title and then changing the remaining words to their canonical form. After treatment, all words in a title are connected to each other, forming a clique. Thus, each title generates a clique, and two cliques are connected if at least one word belongs to two titles (cliques).

Fadigas and Pereira  [11], Pereira et al.  [3] and Henrique et al.  [12] studied semantic clique networks from the titles of articles published in mathematics education journals.

2.3. Network robustness

According to Newman  [13], the theory of percolation in networks aims to understand how the removal of vertices and/or edges affects the network’s topological structure. The percolation of vertices consists of removing a fraction of vertices from a network along with the incident edges at each removed vertex  [13]. The problem of resilience to the random failure of vertices in a network is equivalent to the percolation process of vertices on the network  [14]. In the percolation of edges, the effects of edge removal on the network are investigated. Strategies for vertex and/or edge removal can be defined through criteria such as randomness or higher centrality (e.g., degree and betweenness).

According to Crucitti et al.  [5], the study of a network’s tolerance to attacks and errors has two main objectives: to design new networks as systems that are well integrated into their environment, taking into account only errors or both errors and attacks that may occur, and to protect existing networks, locating critical vertices and taking preventative measures to reduce their criticality. In a similar way, Barabási  [15] argues that to study network robustness (i.e. maintenance of network topological structure when it is subjected to attacks and errors) percolation theory is used to described it.

Chen et al.  [16] argued that the fraction of removed vertices (f) is the ratio of the number of missing or faulty vertices to the number of vertices in the network. Chen et al.  [16] used fragmentation, as defined by Borgatti  [17], i.e.  F=1−Con=2×NCPn(n−1) where Con is the connectivity of a network with n vertices  [18] and NCP is the number of non-connected pairs, to show that it is possible to estimate the percentage of vertices of the giant component (P∞≈1−F) of random and scale-free networks for fractions of removed vertices f less than a critical fraction (i.e., fraction fc of removed vertices at which the network “disintegrates” into small components) when subjected to the random and uniform vertex removal and highest degree vertex removal strategies.

According to Barabási  [15], we assume the concept of robustness is to investigate the impact of the removal of vertices on the integrity of the network.

3. Materials and methods

3.1. Data set

To analyze the robustness of the semantic networks of article titles based on cliques, we used the networks built by Pereira et al.  [3]. The authors built a corpus of sentences from the titles of articles in the following journals: Agricultural and Forest Entomology—AFE; Antipode: A Radical Journal of Geography—ARJG; Applied Psycholinguistics: Psychological and Linguistic Studies Across Languages and Learning—APPL; Chemistry and Biology—CB; Human Relations: Towards the Integration of the Social Sciences—HR; Nature; Physical Review A—PRA; Physical Review B—PRB; Physical Review C—PRC; Physical Review D—PRD; Physical Review E—PRE; Physical Review Letters—PRL; Probabilistic Engineering Mechanics—PEM; Science; and Sociology of Health and Illness—SHI.

3.2. Vertex removal strategies

Our experiment consisted of applying 3 vertex removal strategies to each connected SNT and to the largest component of each disconnected SNT: RUR—random and uniform vertex removal; HDR—highest degree vertex removal; and HBCR—highest betweenness centrality vertex removal. For reasons of convenience, we will call the largest component of the SNT the ‘Journal’ SNT (e.g., AFE SNT; Nature SNT). We used several Network Theory methods to analyze the robustness of each SNT and investigate the topological structure of the networks obtained in the experiment.

In the RUR strategy, we effected 1000 random and uniform vertex removals for each of the following fractions of removed vertices value: 0.05; 0.10; 0.15; …; 0.65; 0.70; 0.71; 0.72; …; 0.97; 0.98; 0.99, such that 43,000 semantic networks were obtained for each journal. This procedure resulted in the creation of 645,000 networks obtained from implementing the RUR strategy. In the HDR strategy, we removed vertices with the highest degree for f=0.01; 0.02; 0.03; …; 0.38; 0.39; 0.40; 0.45; 0.50; …; 0.95, where f is the fraction of removed vertices; for each journal, we obtained 51 semantic networks. A total of 751 networks were obtained in this procedure. In the HBCR strategy, we removed vertices with the highest betweenness centrality for f=0.01; 0.02; 0.03; …until all vertices with nonzero betweenness centrality were removed from the original SNT. We obtained 662 networks subjected to the HBCR strategy.

3.3. Indices of robustness of semantic networks based on cliques

We evaluated the robustness of each SNT subject to the RUR, HDR and HBCR strategies testing the Chen et al.’s method  [16] for the calculation of the size of the largest component of non-scale-free networks and non-random networks, and using a method that we propose for calculation of the average size of the small components.

Initially, we study the behavior of the largest components of an SNT when subjected to the RUR, HDR and HBCR strategies. For this purpose, we modified the proposal of Chen et al.  [16], i.e., P∞≈1−F, considering the number of vertices in the critical fractions. We test Eq. (1) to estimate the size of the largest component in each fraction for an SNT. (1)S(f)n(f)≈1−F(f),for f<fc where n(f) is the number of vertices in the network after removing a fraction f of vertices and S(f) is the number of vertices of the largest component after removing a fraction f of vertices. In the case of fragmentation F(0)=0, trivially we obtain S(0)n(0)=1, where S(0) is the number of vertices of the largest component of the original network (without the removal of vertices) and n(0) is the number of vertices of the original (without the removal of vertices) supposedly connected network.

We verified Eq. (1) for the Science SNT when subject to the RUR, HDR and HBCR strategies for values of f<fc. In the case of the RUR strategy, we made calculations based on 100 networks for each fraction f of removed vertices.

Using the proposed estimate (Eq. (1)), we analyzed the behavior of fragmentation based on empirical results (S(f)n(f)) and on estimates (1−F(f)). We observed that the measures S(f)n(f) and 1−F(f) represent the behavior of the largest component and the network connectivity for each fraction of removed vertices (f), respectively.

The robustness of a network can be examined through the behavior of the size of network’s components after removing a fraction f of the vertices. Thus, the study of the behavior of size of the small components was necessary. The variation of the fraction of vertices of the largest component of the network is the simplest indicator of the resilience in a network; another indicator is the variation in the average size of the small components. We refer to every network component that is smaller than the largest component as the small component.

We evaluated the relationship between the average size of all components of the network (given by 〈T〉(f)) and the average size of the small components of the network (given by 〈s〉(f)) after removing a fraction f of vertices less than the critical fraction fc.

The ratio 〈s〉(f)〈T〉(f) measures the proportion of the average sizes of small components relative to the average size of all components, which is indicative of the relevance of the largest component in the network after the removal of a fraction f of vertices. The ratio 〈s〉(f)〈T〉(f) ranges from zero to one and is zero if the network is connected. When the network’s fragmentation is low, the ratio 〈s〉(f)〈T〉(f) is close to zero. As f approaches the critical fraction fc, the ratio 〈s〉(f)〈T〉(f) tends toward 1. This is because the largest component disintegrates at the critical fraction, and the network then consists of several small components. Therefore, for each SNT subject to the RUR, HDR and HBCR strategies, we can establish the validity of the following relationship: (2)〈s〉(f)〈T〉(f)≈1−S(f)n(f),for f<fc.

Unlike Eq. (1), the ratio 〈s〉(f)〈T〉(f) represents the behavior of the average size of the small components of an SNT in the fraction f.

To estimate S(f)n(0), we use Eq. (1) to obtain the Eq. (3)(3)S(f)n(0)≈n(f)×1−F(f)n(0),for f<fc.

4. Results and discussion

4.1. Determining the critical fraction

For each SNT subjected to the RUR strategy, we observed the existence of a critical fraction, denoted by fcSNT. The criterion adopted for the critical fraction fcSNT to occur consisted of the moment at which the number of vertices of the largest component was less than 1% of the original network, i.e., S(f)n(0)<0.01. According to Barabási  [15], the critical fraction of a random network subjected to the RUR strategy, denoted by fcER, is given by fcER=1−1〈k〉, where 〈k〉 is the average network degree.

The comparison of the values of the critical fractions between each SNT and its respective random network showed that the SNTs are highly robust when subjected to the RUR strategy. The critical fraction of each SNT ranged from 0.96≤fc≤0.98 and of their equivalent random networks ranged from 0.93≤fc≤0.98. We can summarize the relationship between the critical fraction of an SNT and an equivalent random network when subjected to the RUR strategy through Eq. (4). (4)fcSNT≥1−1〈k〉=fcER.

Eq. (4) confirms that the robustness of an SNT subjected to the RUR strategy depends on its average degree; i.e., the higher the average degree is, the more robust the SNT (Table 2).

4.2. Analysis of the impact of vertex removal

The nature of a scale-free network ensures the presence of hubs due to the feature of preferential attachment to vertices of the highest degree. However, the presence of relatively high-degree vertices in a network of cliques does not guarantee that they are hubs. To assess the impact of the removal of vertices of the highest degree in each SNT, we assessed to what extent they correlate with vertices with the highest intermediation centrality. To facilitate this, we analyzed the Nature SNT.

We selected the 21% highest degree vertices (i.e., 4819 vertices of the highest degree) because the critical fraction of the Nature SNT subjected to the HDR strategy was 0.21. Next, the group of 21% of the vertices with the highest intermediation centrality was selected because the critical fraction of the Nature SNT submitted to the HBCR strategy was 0.21. Of the 4819 vertices with the highest degree, 4235 vertices (i.e., approximately 20% of the vertices in the Nature SNT) were in the group of 21% of the vertices with the highest betweenness centrality. It is reasonable to consider these 4235 vertices with the highest degree as hubs, as they are the vertices that most mediate the connection between the remaining pairs of vertices.

We repeated the assessment for the remaining SNTs, taking their respective critical fractions in the HDR strategy, and verified the presence of hubs at a ratio that varied between 18% and 32%. The amount of hubs was related to the robustness of a SNT subjected to the HDR and HBCR strategies.

Therefore, the low resilience of an SNT compared to its respective random networks subjected to the HDR and HBCR strategies is explained by the correlation between words of the highest degree and of the highest betweenness centrality: the removal of a word of the highest degree entails the removal of shortcuts between the words, contributing to the fragmentation of the SNT.

With the RUR strategy, each vertex has the same probability of being removed from the network. Therefore, it is very likely that in each fraction of removed vertices, the proportion of hubs, vertices with betweenness centrality equal to zero and the other vertices in the network will remain. Because there is a higher percentage of vertices with null betweenness centrality, the removal of these vertices will not fragment the network. Because the percentage of hubs is lower than the percentage of vertices with null betweenness centrality, it is very likely for an SNT subjected to the RUR strategy to reach a high fragmentation for f values closer to 1.

In summary, we evaluated the robustness of each SNT subjected to the RUR, HDR and HBCR strategies using Eqs. (1), (2). We calculated the critical fraction fc of each SNT subjected to the RUR, HDR and HBCR strategies and found that SNTs subjected to the RUR strategy were highly robust, while those subjected to the HDR and HBCR strategies had low resilience compared to their respective random networks. In Fig. 4, we show a graphical representation the photographs of the PEM SNT after being subjected to the RUR (Fig. 4(a)), HDR (Fig. 4(b)) and HBCR strategies (Fig. 4(c)).

We observed that the network preserved its topological structure until fraction fp. We selected the largest component of each SNT after the removal of a fraction of vertices and observed that SNTs preserve the small-world phenomenon when subjected to the RUR, HDR and HBCR strategies for f≤fp. In the case of the RUR strategy, we developed a method to check for the presence of the small-world phenomenon: for each SNT and for each fraction f≤fp, we generated a random network by taking the arithmetic mean of the average degree of 1000 networks obtained by random and uniform vertex removal. Comparing the values of C and L of the random network obtained from the arithmetic mean of the average degree of a 1000 networks with the values of C and L of these 1000 networks is equivalent to the method proposed by Watts and Strogatz  [19]. The advantage of our method is that we avoid the requirement to generate 1000 random networks.

5. Conclusions

In this article, we present a study on the structural behavior of clique-based semantic networks when vertex removal strategies are applied. In evaluating the robustness of each SNT subject to the RUR, HDR and HBCR strategies, we noted that the percentage of vertices of the largest component of an SNT can be calculated by Eq. (1) for values of f smaller than the critical fraction.

We presented a method for calculating the size of the small components given by Eq. (2) for values of f smaller than the critical fraction and evaluated the behavior of the size of small components of the networks after removing a fraction f of the vertices.

On the one hand, when subjected to the RUR strategy, SNTs are robust in terms of the values of their critical fractions compared to equivalent random networks. On the other hand, SNTs subjected to the HDR and HBCR strategies feature low robustness compared to random networks.

Finally, we observed that the small-world phenomenon is preserved when subjected to the RUR, HDR and HBCR strategies for f≤fp.
