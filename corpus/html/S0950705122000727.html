1. Introduction

Link prediction has gained popularity in the past decade due to the increasing prominence of network data. Its goal is to infer future links based on the observed networks, which is widely used in a variety of real-world applications, from product recommendations [1] in social networks to interaction discovery [2] in biology, from knowledge graph [3] to graph structure capture [4]. Link prediction algorithms represent an important family of tools for studying the underlying structure of networks. Despite its wide presence, most existing link prediction algorithms are inherently limited to lower-order structure learning and undirected networks [5], [6], [7], and only utilize the network topology information to predict future links. The most intuitive assumption of existing methods is that the more similar of the two nodes, the more likely they have links. Based on this assumption, how to describe and calculate the similarity between nodes become a basic problem of link prediction. Existing methods can be divided into the following two categories according to the way they judge node similarity: (i) traditional topological similarity based methods  [5], [8], [9], [10], and (ii) network representation learning based methods [7], [11], [12], [13]. Traditional topological similarity-based methods predict the link probability according to some local characteristics of the network, such as Jaccard coefficient [9], Sorensen index [14], Leicht–Holme–Newman Index [15], et al. Network representation learning-based methods usually learn the low-dimensional representation vectors of nodes, and then estimate the link probability according to the similarity of their representation vectors. Although many efforts have been devoted to the link prediction (please find detailed introduction in Section 2), two open problems are still poorly understood, which are summarized as follows:

•

Most existing link prediction methods ignore the high-order structure in networks: High-order structure [16] refer to some locally connected components (the number of nodes therein is greater than or equal to 3) in the network, such as triangles, squares, star topological structure, and et al. Benson et al. [16] first introduced the higher-order structure in 2016, and they indicated that more attention should be paid to the local information in the process of link prediction [17]. By analyzing the high-order structure, one can effectively estimate the structural similarity between nodes, and obtain more accurate link prediction results. To better illustrate the higher-order structure, small induced sub-networks called motifs (or graphlets) are considered in [18], [19], [20]. To solve the link prediction problem in directed networks, traditional approaches [21], [22], [23], [24] usually firstly decompose the target directed network into the in-degree and out-degree networks, and then apply the link prediction technologies to each network independently. We remark that this intuitive strategy cannot fully preserve the structure information. As shown in Fig. 1, if we just consider the structure information of in-degree and out-degree networks, nodes C and D can only utilize the topological and attributive information of their common neighbors, i.e., nodes F, G and H, when aggregating network information in the process of link prediction. On the other hand, by considering various motifs in directed networks, one can construct the corresponding motif adjacency matrices to preserve the high-order structure information in the target network. In order to construct the motif adjacency matrix, two nodes are connected if they are co-involved in a particular motif. Based on this principle, nodes C and D can aggregate the information from nodes E, F, G and H, as shown in Fig. 1.

•

Existing link prediction methods mainly focus on undirected networks and ignore the directions of links: To solve the link prediction problem in undirected networks, traditional methods [5], [8], [9], [10] usually estimate the linking probability by calculating the similarity of any two nodes. However, in directed networks, link prediction requires predicting not only the linking probability but also the direction of each link. One cannot inter the direction of a link by only calculating the node similarity, how to predict the directions of links is another challenge for the link prediction in directed networks.

To address the above mentioned challenges, in this paper, we present a novel and powerful autoencoder-based link prediction framework, which utilizes the higher-order structure to learn the linking probability between nodes, and then employs the PageRank centrality to inter the directions of links based on the classic gravitational heuristic formula. Specifically, we first present an efficient motif adjacency matrix learning algorithm, which can be used to extract multiple motifs in directed networks; and then employ GAN (Graph AutoEncoder)-based models to learn node embeddings from each motif adjacency matrix; meanwhile, a robust attention scheme is further introduced to aggregate information of node embeddings learned from multiple motif adjacency matrices to form the final semantically rich node embedding matrix; finally, we adapt the classic gravitational heuristic formula to infer directions of links based on PageRank centrality. The main contributions of this paper are summarized below:

•

We propose a novel encoder scheme to learn node embeddings by using multiple higher-order structure in directed networks. We construct thirteen motif adjacency matrices to retain the high-order structure information in directed networks; and then learn node embeddings by using the graph autoencoder (variational graph auto encoder).

•

We propose a robust attention scheme to aggregate node embeddings learned from multiple motif adjacency matrices. In order to aggregate node embeddings from different motif adjacency matrices, we propose an attention-based aggregation scheme to adaptively learn the weights of motifs in the target directed network, which can significantly improve the effectiveness of linking probability estimation.

•

We propose an efficient decoder scheme to predict the directions of links by using the PageRank centrality. In order to handle the link prediction problem in directed networks, we utilize the node centrality information (i.e., PageRank centrality) to design an efficient decoder scheme, which can effectively infer the directions of links based the classic gravitational heuristic formula.

•

Model and Evaluation. We propose a novel and powerful link prediction framework for directed networks, named MGAE, and further extend it to the variational graph autoencoder version (MVGAE). Extensive experiments were applied on various types of directed networks to validate the performance of our proposed approach through comparisons with ten state-of-the-art link prediction technologies.

The rest of this article is organized as follows. In Section 2, we review some most related works. Section 3 states the problem formulation in detail. Section 4 introduces some preliminaries before presenting our method. The proposed link prediction framework is presented in Section 5. Section 6 shows the experimental results. Finally, we conclude the article and give guidelines for the future work in Section 7.

2. Related work

Link prediction is essential to dig out the reasons and driving forces for the connections among nodes in networks, which can be intuitively understood as to measure the node similarity. The more similar of the two nodes, the more likely they have links. Existing methods of link prediction can be roughly divided into two categories: traditional topological similarity based methods and network representation learning based methods. In the following subsections, we review some representative link prediction methods.

2.1. Topological similarity based methods

Link prediction has been historically addressed through graph mining heuristics, traditional topological similarity based methods predict the linking probability by mining the topology structure in the network. They can be divided into three categories based on the topological information they consider: (i) neighbor based, (ii) path-based, and (iii) random walk based approaches.

Common Neighbor (CN) [5] is the most widely used link prediction method, and it has low computational complexity and strong interpretability. This measure uses the size of intersection of two nodes’ neighbor sets to measure their topological similarity. CN follows the strategy that, the more co-neighbors between two nodes, the more likely there is a link between them. Jaccard Coefficient (JC) [9] improves CN by considering the total number of neighbors. It is in fact a normalized version of CN. Adamic and Adar (AA) [25] is another link prediction method, which can improve the prediction accuracy by using the degree to distinguish the contributions of different common neighbors. A significant problem with these methods is that they cannot effectively handle the directed networks. In order to retain the directionality of links, Laishram [26] proposed an improved method by considering either the common in-degree neighbors or the common out-degree neighbors. However, in directed networks, only considering the common neighbors is often not enough for link prediction. To this end, Ghorbanzadeh et al. [27] proposed a link prediction method based on common neighbor and motifs. They consider the number of common neighbors and two specific motifs called hub and authority. Aghabozorgi and Khayyambashi [28] proposed a new link prediction measure, which combined the common neighbor similarity and triad similarity between nodes. Triad similarity is obtained by counting the number of co-participating motifs between nodes. Katz [8] is a path-dependent method, which follows the strategy that the more accessible paths between the two nodes, the greater linking probability between them. Average Commute Time (ACT) [10] is a typical method based on random walk. It assumes that the smaller sum of the average steps will lead into higher linking probability between nodes.

2.2. Network representation learning based methods

Network representation learning (NRL) methods aim to reduce the dimension of high-dimensional graph data into a lower-dimensional representation. NRL-based methods take the link prediction as a binary classification problem by considering the presence or absence of links between nodes. Both topological information and attributive information are considered by NRL-based methods, which can aggregate two types of information to obtain the representation vectors of nodes. The linking probability is inversely proportional to the distance between nodes’ representation vectors. According to the way they compute the representation vector, NRL-based methods can be categorized into three types: (i) random walk based, (ii) matrix Factorization based, and (iii) graph neural network based methods.

Random walk based methods sample a network by starting walking from random initial nodes. The randomness of walks gives the ability to explore the network and capture both the local and global structural information. After the walk paths are built, probability models like skip-gram can be performed on these randomly sampled paths to learn the node embeddings. DeepWalk [6] is a method based on network topology information, which uses Word2vec [11] scheme and random walk to aggregate network topology information as the node representation vectors. Node2vec [29] improves DeepWalk by considering the second-order similarity of nodes. In DeepWalk, sampled sequences are based on the depth-first sampling (DFS) strategy. However, if the contextual sequences are sampled by the DFS strategy alone, only a few nodes close to the source node can be sampled. Different from DFS, the breadth-first sampling (BFS) strategy can explore more neighboring nodes. As a result, Node2vec proposes a probability model in which the random walk has a certain probability to revisit nodes being traveled before. Matrix factorization, also called graph factorization (GF), factorizes the adjacency matrix of a network to obtain the node embeddings. It corresponds to a structure-preserving dimensionality reduction process. Yang et al. [30] proved that DeepWalk can be considered as a matrix factorization operation and proposed a method called Text-associated DeepWalk (TADW), it incorporates text features of nodes into network representation learning under the framework of matrix factorization. GraRep [31], aiming to preserve the higher-order proximity of networks in the embedding space, obtains a kth order transition matrix by multiplying the adjacency matrix to itself k times. Chen et al. [32] utilized an asymmetric link clustering method to calculate the link clustering coefficient and retained the local network structure information; by jointly optimizing them in the nonnegative matrix factorization model, it can preserve both the local and global information at the same time. Recently, graph neural network (GNN) is widely used in network representation learning and has achieved good experimental results. The link prediction methods based on GNN often includes an encoder framework and a decoder mechanism. The node embeddings are learned by an encoder, and the link prediction is performed in a decoder according to the similarity of the node embeddings. Graph autoencoder (GAE) [7] is a method based on Graph Neural Network (GNN). GAE uses GCN as the encoder, which makes full use of network topology information and attributes information. Since GAE has been proposed, many autoencoder based approaches [12], [13], [24], [33], [34], [35], [36] have emerged. To extend GAE to directed networks, Salha [21] proposed a gravity-inspired decoding approach, named Gravity Inspired Graph Auto-encoder (Gravity GAE), which is inspired by the gravitational pull between planets. Gravity GAE follows the strategy that the linking probability is determined by the similarity of nodes, and links always lead from the high mass to the low mass. This method provides a way to predict the directions of links. Ahn and Kim [37] proposed a link prediction method, which utilizes 2-normalization to obtain better node embeddings in sparse networks.

3. Problem definition

In this section, we formally define the link prediction problem in directed networks. The main symbols used in this paper are listed in Table 1.

Given a directed network G=〈A,F〉, where A∈Rn×n denotes the (weighted) adjacency matrix and F∈Rn×d represents the feature matrix, let D∈Rn×n denote the diagonal matrix of node degrees. Note that in the directed network G, if there exists a directed link (i.e., eij) from node i to node j, we have Aij=1. The out-degree and in-degree of node i are defined as kiout and kiin, respectively. As an important topic of graph data mining, link prediction is intended to predict the possibility of future links between any two nodes in the network by analyzing existing network information [38]. Fig. 2 shows the differences of link prediction in undirected networks and directed networks. The link prediction in the directed networks need to predict the potential links in the network and infer the link direction according to the topological information and attributive information in the networks. We usually hide some links of the network as input, and then employ the link prediction approach to reconstruct the network. The more similar between the predicted network and original one, the better the link prediction approach is. With the above notions, link prediction in directed networks can be formally defined as follows:

4. Preliminaries

Before introducing our link prediction method, we begin with some preliminaries. First, a typical graph autoencoder [7] is reviewed, which can be used to learn node embeddings in undirected network; and then an extended version of GAE, named Gravity GAE [21], is reviewed, which can be used to infer the directions of links in directed networks.

4.1. GAE & VGAE

Graph autoencoders (GAEs) are a family of unsupervised models which extend autoencoders to graph structure. It is a specific application of the network representation learning method (Graph Convolutional Network, GCN) in the field of link prediction. A typical GAE is composed of two stacked models:

•

A GCN encoder [7]. Its goal is to learn the node embeddings, i.e., the low dimensional representation vectors of nodes in the network. A two-layer GCN encoder aggregates information according to the following rules: (1)GCN(A,F)=ÃReLU(ÃFW0)W1where W0 and W1 denote the first-layer weight matrix and the second-layer weight matrix, respectively. Ã=D−12AD−12 is the symmetrically normalized adjacency matrix and ReLU(⋅)=max(0,⋅).

•

An Inner Decoder. The output of the GCN encoder can be viewed as the node embedding matrix (i.e., X=GCN(A,F)) of the original network, then an inner decoder is employed to reconstruct the original adjacency matrix A as follows: (2)Aˆ=φ(XX⊤).where φ(⋅) is the activation function, Aˆij equals 1 if (XX⊤)ij is greater than 0.5, and 0 otherwise.

Variational Graph AutoEncoder (VGAE) [7] is a combination of Variational Bayesian and GAE. The encoder is an inference model parameterized by a two-layer GCN: (3)q(X∣A,F)=∏i=1nq(xi∣A,F)where q(xi∣A,F) is chosen from the Gaussian distribution Normal(μi,diag(σ)i2), in which μ=GCNμ(A,F) is the matrix containing all mean vectors μi, σ=GCNσ(A,F) is the matrix containing all variance vectors σi. GCNμ(A,F) and GCNσ(A,F) share the first-layer weight matrix W0.

4.2. Gravity GAE & Gravity VGAE

The inner product decoder of GAE/VGAE suffers from a challenge issue in directed networks: the directions of links cannot be determined by the inner product of the embedding vectors. Salha et al. [21] improve GAE by proposing a new decoder called gravity-inspired decoder. To extend GAE to handle the link prediction problem in directed networks, Salha et al. [21] borrow the idea from Newton theory of universal gravitation, and consider the nodes in networks as the celestial bodies in space. To be specific, the gravity between nodes (celestial bodies) i and j can be defined as (4)gravityij=Gmimjrij2where G is the gravitational constant, mi and mj are the masses of nodes (celestial bodies) i and j, respectively. rij denotes the distance between of the two nodes (celestial bodies). Then the centripetal acceleration from node i to node j, i.e., ai→j is defined as (5)ai→j=gravityijmi=Gmjrij2Similarly, the centripetal acceleration from node j to node i is (6)aj→i=gravityjimj=Gmirij2

If we view nodes in directed networks as celestial bodies in space, such centripetal acceleration can be viewed as the linking probability from node i to node j, i.e., pˆij. Salha et al. [21] drew inspiration directly from this theory, especially from the formulation of acceleration, to build the proposed decoder model:

•

Low-influential nodes tend to link to high-influential nodes. For example, in a citation network, ordinary articles tend to cite highly cited articles.

•

Nodes with high structural proximity are more likely to be connected. With the network representation learning, the distance rij2 in Eq. (5) can be viewed as squared Euclidean distance between the embedding vectors of the two nodes, such that (7)rij2=‖xi−xj‖2

Instead of directly dealing with ai→j, Gravity GAE uses logai→j, which limits the potentially large values resulting from acceleration towards central nodes. In the reconstructed adjacency matrix Aˆ, Gravity GAE utilizes the following strategy to inter the directed links: (8)Aˆij=φ(logai→j)=φ(logGmj−log‖xi−xj‖2)=φ(mˆj−log‖xi−xj‖2)where mˆj=logGmj is viewed as a learning parameter, φ(⋅) is the activation function, Aˆij equals 1 if logai→j is greater than 0.5, and 0 otherwise.

5. Methodology

In this section, we proposed a novel autoencoder framework to address the link prediction in directed networks. It has the following advantages: (i) It considers the higher-order structure in the network. (ii) It can predict the directions of links. The proposed motif-based link prediction framework, as shown in Fig. 3, also depends on the autoencoder structure, which consists of two essential components: (i) a robust encoder scheme to learn node embeddings by considering multiple higher-order structure in directed networks, (ii) an efficient decoder scheme to reconstruct the target directed network by utilizing node embeddings and PageRank centrality.

5.1. Motif-based graph autoencoder

Given a directed network G=〈A,F〉, the encoder in the Motif-based Graph Autoencoder (MGAE) contains three essential components. First, the encoder constructs the kth motif adjacency matrix Ak based on the network topological information, which represents a specific higher-order relationship between nodes. Second, MGAE uses the GCN model to learn the representation vectors of nodes. For each motif presented, an embedding matrix Xk can be obtained according to the above process. Third, the attention mechanism is utilized to aggregate node embeddings learned from multiple motif adjacency matrices and obtain the final node embedding matrix X. The decoder uses the final node embedding matrix to predict the directed links and outputs a reconstructed directed network Gˆ=〈Aˆ,Fˆ〉.

5.2. Motif adjacency matrix

Existing GAE-based methods have achieved good prediction accuracy in undirected networks, but they still have some drawbacks. First, they do not consider the higher-order structure in networks. Second, they cannot inter the directions o links. To address these two drawbacks, we adopt a motif-based method to extend GAE to handle directed networks.

Motifs are small, connected, non-isomorphic induced subnetworks in networks. Each motif represents a topological pattern of interconnection between κ nodes. There are totally 13 motifs with κ=3 nodes in directed networks, as shown in the top of Fig. 4. Instances of motifs with up to 4 nodes are also shown in bottom of Fig. 4. In this paper, all 3-node motif (denoted by M1,…,M13) in directed networks are considered.

For a given directed network G=〈A,F〉 and the kth motif Mk, we estimate the frequency of Mk by exploring and capturing more higher-order structure. To count the relationships between each node pair, the definition of Motif Neighbor is defined as follows:

Definition 1 Motif Neighbor

Given a directed network G=〈A,F〉 and a motif Mk, the framework forms a motif adjacency matrix (Ak) by counting the number of two nodes co-occur in an instance of Mk. For a node pair (i,j), if there exists a subnetwork containing nodes i and j in the network G, and it has exactly the same structure as Mk, node j is the motif neighbor of node i, and the number of such subnetworks is called the motif neighbor weight wij.

The kth motif adjacency matrix Ak represents the distribution information of the k-motif (i.e., Mk) in the directed network. Algorithm 1 provides an efficient method to calculate the motif adjacency matrix. For each node pair (i,j), if there is a link between them, then we search for a third node s among the rest nodes that are linked to them. Then, we compare the subnetworks (i,j,s) with each motif, and update the corresponding motif adjacency matrix Ak, as shown in Lines 5–13 of Algorithm 1. During the motif matching process, a simple pruning strategy is utilized as follows: if the number of links in the subnetwork equals to 3, we only need to compared (i,j,s) with M1,M5,M11,M12.

The symmetric motif adjacency matrix contains higher-order structure information. The symmetry of the adjacency matrix is one of the prerequisites for using GCN. In what following, the kth motif adjacency matrix can be sometimes written as (9)Ak=MAML(A,Mk)

5.3. Encoder–decoder model

Although both GAE and VGEA show good results in undirected networks, they cannot be directly employed to address the link prediction problem in directed networks. To this end, we propose an improved encoder as follows.

5.3.1. Motif-based graph AutoEncoder (MGAE)

In this subsection, we present the details of the Motif-based Graph AutoEncoder (MGAE) to handle the link prediction in directed networks. According to Section 5.2, we can use Algorithm 1 to obtain 13 motif adjacency matrices. For each motif adjacency matrix, MGAE adopts the GCN-based method to obtain the node embedding matrix. In the subsequent process of representation learning, we need to consider the characteristics of the node itself, so we update the motif adjacency matrix A by self-loop. (10)Ãk=Ak+Iwhere I is the identity matrix with size n×n.

Then, the final node embedding matrix X can be learned by utilizing a GCN encoder. In such encoder, we use each motif adjacency matrix to represent the adjacency relationship among nodes, and adopt a two-layer GCN model, as shown in Eq. (1), to learn the node embedding matrix from a particular motif adjacency matrix. Finally, the attention mechanism is employed to aggregate multiple node embedding matrices: (11)X=∑k=1KBkXkwhere K is the total number of motifs we consider, Bk is the weight of motif Mk, which satisfies that ∑k=1KBk=1. Xk is the node embedding matrix learned from the kth motif adjacency matrix Ak using the GCN model: (12)Xk=GCN(Ãk,F)

With the above encoder scheme, we can obtain the low dimensional representation vector of each node, i.e., xi. For the link prediction task in directed networks, we further present a novel decoder scheme to predict the further direct links in the networks. By using the node embedding matrix and node centrality, the probability that there is a directed link from node i to node j is defined as (13)pˆij=σ(cj−λlog(‖xi−xj‖2))where cj is the centrality of the target node j, λ is a parameter to balance the effects of the pairwise proximity and the node centrality. σ(⋅) is the sigmoid function.

Salha et al. [21] first proposed to use the last dimension of the embedding matrix X as the node influence and achieved good experimental results. However, this method is inexplicable, it is difficult to explain why the last dimension can be used as the node centrality. As an important attribute in complex network analysis, node centrality has many measurement methods. In this paper, we use PageRank [39] algorithm, which is the most widely used one, to calculate node centrality. PageRank uses a random browsing model and follows the strategy that: (14)ciPR=1−dn+d∑j∈NiincjPR|Njout|where ciPR is the PageRank centrality of node i, n is the total number of nodes, 1−d is the random jumping probability, Niin is the set of in-degree neighbors of node i, Njout is the set of out-degree neighbors of node j. After several iterations, we can get the PageRank centrality of each node and obtain the following centrality vector: (15)cPR=1−dn(I−dADˇ−1)−11where Dˇ∈Rn×n denotes the diagonal matrix of node out-degrees, with each element Dˇii=max(kiout,1), and 1 is an n-dimensional vector of all ones.

The goal of MGAE is to minimize the gap between the reconstructed adjacency matrix Aˆ and the original matrix A. The specific parameters that should be learned include the weight matrix W0k and W1k in the two-layer GCN model (see Eq. (1)) and the weight Bk in the encoder (see Eq. (11)) for each motif Mk. 5.4. Complexity analysis

Given a directed network G=〈A,F〉 with n nodes and m links, let 〈k〉=2mn denote the average degree of G. Z∈Rn×d1 is the hidden layer in GCN model. X∈Rn∗d2 is the embedding matrix of network G. The complexity of MGAE/MVGAE is mainly analyzed in two parts :(i) the process of constructing motif adjacency matrices and (ii) encoder–decoder model.

The process of constructing the kth motif adjacency matrix has been described in detail in Algorithm 1, where we first traverse each three-node connected subnetwork (see Lines 4–6), the time cost of it is O(n〈k〉2). For each subnetwork, we need to compare with the K=13 motifs as shown in the top of Fig. 4. By employing the pruning strategy, we can reduce the number of comparisons from 13 to 4. The total cost of learning motif adjacency matrices can be written as O(n〈k〉2). The time complexity of encoder–decoder model is the sum of the cost of three parts, which is shown in Algorithm 2. First, The total time consumed by GCN encoder is O(mdd1d2) (see Lines 5–17). Second, the total time of attention mechanism is O(n2) (Lines 18). Finally, the time cost of decoder is O(d2n2) (Lines 20–22). The time complexity of encoder–decoder model is O(d2n2+mdd1d2). Therefore, the overall time complexity of MGAE/MVGAE is O(n〈k〉2+d2n2+mdd1d2).

6.1. Experimental setup

Datasets Four real-world benchmark directed networks, including Cora [40], Citeseer [40], Bitcoin [41], [42] and Google [21], are used in the experiments, among which, Cora and Citeseer are two citation networks, Bitcoin is a trading network, and Google belongs to the web network. Some basic statistics of the experimental datasets are shown in Table 2, where n and m denote the numbers of nodes and directed links, w.r.t. each benchmark network. More detail information of these four datasets are summarized as follows:

•

Cora [40] is a citation network, which consists of 2708 nodes (scientific publications) and 5429 directed links (citations).

•

Citeseer [40] also belongs to the citation network, where nodes are scientific publications and directed links represent citations.

•

Bitcoin [41], [42] is a who-trusts-whom network of people who trade on a platform called Bitcoin. Since Bitcoin users are anonymous, there is a need to maintain a record of users’ reputation to prevent transactions with fraudulent and risky users

•

Google [21] is a web network consisting of 15763 web pages, where the directed links indicate hyperlinks among different web pages.

Parameters Setting. Both MGAE and MVGAE are defaultly trained for 200 epochs, unless the user-specified termination condition (see Lines 29–31 in Algorithm refalg:2) meets, then they will return d2=32-dimensional node representations vectors. To accelerate training process, if the difference between the tth epoch loss and the t−10th epoch loss is less than 0.05, both MGAE and MVGAE will be prematurely terminated. For the sparse networks, such as Cora, Citeseer and Bitcoin, the regulating parameter λ that determines the density of the reconstructed network, is specified as 2; and for the dense network Google, we set λ=10. For all benchmark networks, we randomly selected 10% node pairs as the training set (PTrain), 10% node pairs as the verification set (PVarify), and the rest 80% ones as the test set (PTest). Note that, the node pairs in PTrain are utilized to train the weight matrices in GCNs (i.e., ∀k∈[1,K],W0k,W1k) and the weights in the attentional mechanism (i.e., ∀k∈[1,K],Bk); the node pairs in PVarify are used to learn the hyper-parameters, such as the regulating parameter λ and the dimension of node embedding vector d2; finally, we make use of node pairs in PTest to compare the effectiveness of different link prediction approaches.

Evaluation Metrics. To measure the alignment between the predicted node pairs in PPrediction and the ground-truth node pairs in PTest, we employ the following five metrics, all of which were widely used in existing literature of link prediction [7], [21], [34], [36]. Let P+Prediction={(i,j)|(i,j)∈PPrediction,Aˆij=1} and P−Prediction={(i,j)|(i,j)∈PPrediction,Aˆij=0} denote the positive and negative samples, learned by the link prediction model, respectively; and let P+Test={(i,j)|(i,j)∈PTest,Aij=1} and P−Test={(i,j)|(i,j)∈PTest,Aij=0} denote the ground-truth positive and negative samples in PTest. The sets of True Positive (TP), True Negative (TN), False Positive (FP) and False Negative (FN), can be denoted by PTP=P+Prediction∩P+Test, PTN=P−Prediction∩P−Test, PFP=P+Prediction∩P−Test and PFN=P−Prediction∩P+Test, respectively. With the above notations, the five evaluation metrics used in this article can be formally defined as follows:

Baseline Tools. Ten well-known link prediction algorithms are selected as baselines for comparison:

•

SDNE [43], which is designed for the link prediction in undirected networks, employs an autoencoder structure to simultaneously optimize the first-order and second-order similarities, the learned node embedding vectors can retain both local and global structure. The first-order similarity refers to the similarity of the node embedding vectors of nodes with link connected; while the second-order similarity refers to the similarity between two nodes that have common neighbors but are not directly connected.

•

SCNHA [27] is a recently proposed link prediction tool for undirected networks, which belongs to the family of traditional topological similarity-based methods. It considers not only common neighbors, but also two high-order structure in the network, i.e., hub and authority. In this approach, two higher-order structure and common neighbors are counted separately, to measure the pairwise proximity.

•

GAE [7] is the well-known link prediction for undirected networks. In order to employ GAE to handle the link prediction task in directed networks, it divides the original adjacency matrix into upper and lower triangle matrices, and takes the symmetrical operation to obtain two undirected networks (in-degree and out-degree versions). Then GAE further employs the two-layer GCNs as the encoder to learn the node embedding matrices from the in-degree and out-degree networks, respectively. Finally, an inner-product-based decoder is used to predict the in-degree and out-degree links in the original directed network, respectively.

•

VGAE [7] extends GAE to the Variational Autoencoder version. The main difference between GAE and VGAE lies at the encoder part, the later adopts Variational Bayesian and graph autoencoder to learn the node embedding matrix.

•

VGNAE [37] is also an extended version of GAE. It proposes to normalize the node embedding matrix and considers isolated nodes in the network. This strategy can significantly improve GAE’s performance on sparse networks.

•

NMF-AP [32] is a novel matrix factorization-based method for link prediction in undirected networks. It uses asymmetric link clustering method to calculate the link clustering coefficient and retains the local network structure information. In this way, it achieves better results than other matrix factorization-based methods.

•

Triadic [28] is the undirected link prediction method based on motif extraction. It considers 13 kinds of triangular motifs to calculate the pairwise proximity of any two nodes, and finally adopts a weighted normalized index to measure the similarity between nodes. Like traditional topological similarity-based methods, it follows the principle that the greater the similarity of nodes, the greater the probability of linking.

•

DeepWalk [6] is the classical network representation method, which learns the node representation vectors by utilizing based on random walk. As it cannot directly solve the link prediction problem in (un)directed networks, we ignored the directionality of links in the benchmark datasets and consider the inner product of node embedding vectors as the pairwise proximity to infer interconnected links in networks.

•

gravity GAE [21] is designed for link prediction in directed networks, which also divides the adjacency matrix into upper and lower triangle matrices and lets them to be symmetric. The main difference between GAE and gravity GAE is that the later utilizes the node centrality to infer the directions of links in the reconstructed network.

•

gravity VGAE [21] extends gravity GAE to the Variational Autoencoder version. Their main difference lies at the encoder part. Like VGAE, gravity VGAE adopts Variational Bayesian and graph autoencoder to learn the node embedding matrix. Note that both gravity GAE and gravity VGAE use the last dimension of node embedding matrix to measure the node centrality (see Fig. 5).

6.2. Performance comparison

In this subsection, we validate the effectiveness of our proposed models (MGAE and MVGAE) by comparing them with different types of baseline tools. Considering the effect of stochastic factors, the results obtained by all test approaches report the average prediction accuracy over 30 runs on each network. We adopt the same parameter settings for the same type of baseline tools. For example, the methods based graph neural network, such as GAE, VGAE, gravity GAE, gravity VGAE, MGAE and MVGAE, use the same learning rate and training epochs. Comparison on prediction quality in terms of the five evaluation metrics is shown in Fig. 5, from which, the following conclusions can be observed:

•

Different algorithms obtain the similar performance trends in terms of AUC and AP on each benchmark network, respectively. The proposed MVGAE, considering both high-order structure and node centrality, performs best on all networks except Cora. Both MGAE and MVGAE are slightly inferior to gravity VGAE but perform better than the others on Cora. All the test tools except gravity GAE/VGAE and MGAE/MVGAE, are designed for link prediction in undirected networks, the output positive samples are those node pairs with mutual directed links, which result into the poor performance on AUC and AP. Moreover, the regulating parameter λ in gravity GAE/VGAE and MGAE/MVGAE is capable to adjust the number of positive samples in the final reconstructed network. By setting an appropriate λ, both AUC and AP can be improved by GAE/VGAE and MGAE/MVGAE.

•

In term of RECALL, by considering isolated nodes and normalizing the node embedding matrix, VGNAE performs best on all benchmark networks except Citeseer. Compared with gravity GAE/VGAE and MGAE/MVGAE, the rest test algorithms cannot control the size of prediction set, the resulted outputs usually include a large number of predicted samples; in other words, those methods will sacrifice AUC/AP at the improvement of RECALL. Our proposed MGAE and MVGAE achieve the best measure quality on Citeseer, and always stand as one of the best-three options on Bitcoin and Google.

•

The proposed MGAE and MVGAE perform best in term of F1 on all datasets except Cora, implying that our link prediction approach can achieve a good balance on PRECISION and RECALL. By consider triangular motifs in networks, Triadic obtains the highest F1 score on Cora, followed by gravity VGAE and MGAE. Compared with other GNN-based prediction approaches, i.e., GAE, VGAE, VGNAE, gravity GAE and gravity VGAE, our approach utilizes multiple motifs to learn node embedding vectors and infers directions of links based on PageRank centrality, therefore has better performance.

•

Finally, in term of NMI, the proposed MGAE and MVGAE achieve the best performance on Cora and Citeseer; while on those dense networks, i.e., Bitcoin and Google, they are inferior to gravity GAE and gravity VGAE but perform better than the others. We remark that both Bitcoin and Google contain a larger proportion of motif M10 (see Fig. 4), in other words, these two networks include a large number of central nodes with high in-degree. With this characteristic, some important structural information could be easily input into the upper and lower triangle matrices by both gravity GAE and gravity VGAE, therefore they perform better than MGAE and MVGAE.

6.3. Parameter analysis

In this subsection, we choose Citeseer and employ the GridSearchCV to learn the default parameter settings of λ and d2 in MVGAE. To be specific, we specify λ and d2 to be {14,12,1,2,4,8,16} and {16,32,64,128,256}, respectively. Fig. 6 reports the changes of five evaluation metrics w.r.t. different parameter combinations. In each sub-figure, the horizontal x-axis indicates the distribution of λ; and the vertical y-axis reflects the distribution of d2.

The effect of parameter λ. Note that parameter λ in Eq. (13) can control the number of positive samples in the prediction set PPrediction; the larger setting of λ could result into a smaller number of positive samples and a larger number of positive samples otherwise. As shown in Fig. 6, both AUC and AP are improving with the increase of λ. This implies that MVGAE can achieve higher AUC and AP when the reconstructed directed network is of lower link density. Note that when λ is set to be a very large value, the reconstructed directed network becomes very sparse; in this situation, most linked node pairs in the original network cannot be found by MVGAE, which will result into the lower RECALL. As shown in Fig. 6, with the increase of λ, other evaluation metrics, i.e., RECALL, F1 and NMI, are dropping continuously. Therefore, to balance the precision and recall of MVGAE, we set λ=2 by default in the experiments.

The effect of parameter d2. The parameter d2 determines the dimension of resulted node embedding vectors. Empirically, a higher-dimensional representation vector can describe each node’s characteristics more comprehensively. However, it is not true in the real situation, as shown in Fig. 6. All the five evaluation metrics show to be not very sensitive to the change of parameter d2. With the increase of d2 from 16 to 256, the values of most metrics are rising first and then falling when d2 exceeds 64. This implies small values of d2 could reduce the representation ability, while large values of d2 could result into the expensive training cost of MVGAE. Therefore, to balance the effectiveness and efficiency of MVGAE, we set d2=32 by default in the following experiments.

6.4. Convergence analysis

Here, we illustrate the convergence of MVGAE by observing the trend of Loss during each epoch in Algorithm 2. Due to the page limit, we choose Citeseer in this experiment. During the training process, we not only record the value of Loss, but also the scores of the five selected evaluation metrics, in each epoch. Note that, the scores of evaluation metrics are calculated using the samples in the verification set PVarify. MVGAE adopts the Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) strategy to update the internal parameters, i.e., ∀k∈[1,K],W0k,W1k,Bk. Theoretically, the value of Loss will continuously drop with the increase of epoch. Fig. 7 shows the trends of Loss value, as well as, the scores of five evaluation metrics. The Loss curve has a very significant downward trend at the beginning of training, while at the same time, all indicator curves have a very similar upward trend, i.e., the scores keep improving continuously regardless of the evaluation metric adopted. After 40–50 epochs of training, the downward trend of Loss tends to be flat, however the values of all evaluation metrics show to decrease with the increase of training epoch. This implies that there could be over-fitting in model training. To solve this issue, we introduce a heuristic based termination condition as shown in Lines 29–31 of Algorithm 2.

6.5. Module analysis inside MGAE & MVGAE

In this subsection, we attempt to employ the fixed module strategy to analyze the impact of the three functional modules inside MGAE and MVGAE, which are higher-order structure learning (Lines 1–3 in Algorithm 2), motif-based attentional mechanism (Line 18 in Algorithm 2) and PageRank centrality calculation (Line 19 in Algorithm 2). We compare eight variants of MGAE/MVGE with the proposed full-stack MGAE/MVGAE on Citeseer:

•

MGAE/MVGAE without extracting higher-order structure (MGAE-woMotif & MVGE-woMotif): We replace Lines 1–3 in Algorithm 2 by extracting the in-degree and out-degree adjacency matrices.

•

MGAE/MVGAE without attentional mechanism (MGAE-woATT & MVGE-woATT): We replace Line 18 in Algorithm 2 by treating each motif equally (Bk=1K).

•

MGAE/MVGAE without PageRank centrality (MGAE-woPR & MVGE-woPR): We replace Line 19 in Algorithm 2 by using the last-dimension value of node embedding vector as its centrality.

•

MGAE/MVGAE using in-degree centrality (MGAE-inDegree & MVGE-inDegree): We replace Line 19 in Algorithm 2 by using node’s in-degree as its centrality.

The performance comparison of the above eight variant algorithms and MGAE/MVGAE is summarized in Table 3. Broadly speaking, all VGAE-based models are better than GAE-based ones, implying that fusing variational Bayesian and GAE can learn better node embedding matrix. In terms of AUC and AP, MVGAE is slightly inferior to MGAE-woPR but performs better than the other variants. By using the last-dimension value of node embedding vector as its centrality, the reconstructed adjacency matrix obtained by MGAE-woPR is very sparse. Although MGAE-woPR achieves the highest scores on both AUC and AP, its performance on the other three metrics is the worst. The proposed full-stack MVGAE performs best on RECALL, F1 and NMI, followed by MGAE and MVGAE-inDegree. With the proposed higher-order structure extraction module, MVGAE can boost the performance by over 15 percent on F1 and 112 percent on NMI, compared with MVGAE-woMotif; meanwhile, by adopting the attentional mechanism to aggregate multiple embedding matrices learned from different motif networks, MVGAE improves the prediction performance by over 18 percent on F1 and 70 percent on NMI, compared with MVGAE-woATT; finally, by using PageRank centrality to determine the directions of links, MVGAE increases its performance by over 36 percent on F1 and 85 percent on NMI, compared with MVGAE-woPR. These results also demonstrate the effectiveness of the three modules inside MGAE/MVGAE, i.e., higher-order structure learning, attentional mechanism and PageRank centrality.

6.6. Empirical analysis of motifs in real-world networks

In this subsection, we present the empirical analysis of motifs in real-world benchmark directed networks. Recall that by employing the attentional mechanism (see Line 18 in Algorithm 2), we can learn the weight of each motif during the training process. If a given motif Mk’s weight exceeds the average value (i.e., Bk>1K), we say that such a motif Mk has a positive effect towards link prediction task. At the same time, we can also record the frequency of occurrence of different motifs in the networks. Fig. 8 reports the weight and frequency distributions of different motifs in the four real-world benchmark networks, from which we can observe that:

•

The higher frequency of a motif, the more important it may affect the node embedding vectors. As shown in Fig. 8(a) and (b), both motifs M8 and M10 frequently appear in Core and Citeseer, their weights, i.e., B8 and B10, both exceed 113; and the weights of high-frequency motifs M11 and M13 in Bitcoin also larger than 113, as shown in Fig. 8(c). However, there is a counter-example as shown in Fig. 8(d), the weight of high-frequency motif M10 in Google has been below average, implying that our link prediction approach cannot fully use motif M10 in Google to learn node embedding matrix. As a web network, the node in Google indicates the webpage and the directed link represents the hyperlink from the source webpage to the target webpage. Recent literature [44] shows link prediction in web networks will pay more attention on the transitive relationship, such as M8 and M9, rather than the convergence relationship like M10. To this end, we ignore M10 in Google, and relearn the weigh distribution of the rest 3-node motifs. The results shown in Fig. 9 indicate that the weights of high-frequency motifs M8 and M9 in Google both exceed the average level 112, which is consistent with the findings in other networks.

•

The frequency distributions of motifs in real-world benchmark directed networks are different. In this work, we mainly consider three types of real-world directed networks. Both Cora and Citeseer are citation networks, which are significantly affected by M8 and M10. Nodes (articles) in citation networks can be divided into two categories, i.e., authoritative nodes and ordinary ones. There are fewer mutually citations between authoritative articles and authoritative articles are often cited by multiple independently associated ordinary articles, such two citation patterns can be well described by M8 and M10, as shown in Fig. 4. The trading network Bitcoin can be viewed as a special social network, where the transitivity of trust plays an important role. The classic social balance theory shows that “only loops of even number of negative links can ensure the structural balance in directed signed networks”, if a pair of nodes has mutually directed links, they are more likely to link to the third node whom is friend with any of them. Such connection patterns can be captured by M11 and M13, as shown in Fig. 4. Finally, there is the significant hierarchical structure in the web network Google: some navigation webpages link to multiple information webpages, this can be well captured by M8; and the phenomenon of content transfer from one webpage to the other can be detected by M9.

7. Conclusion

In this paper, we propose a novel and powerful autoencoder framework, which can effectively integrate both high-order structure and node centrality in directed networks for link prediction. We first provide an efficient motif adjacency matrix learning algorithm, which can be used to extract high-order structure in directed networks, and construct multiple motif adjacency matrices. Then, we carefully study a novel autoencoder model, which consists of three core modules. (i) First (Node Embedding): a novel encoder scheme is presented to learn the node embeddings from each motif adjacency matrix; (ii) Second (Information Fusion): a robust attention scheme is further introduced to aggregate information of node embeddings learned from multiple motif adjacency matrices to form the final semantically rich node embedding matrix; (iii) Third (Link Prediction): an efficient decoder scheme is finally proposed to reconstruct the target directed network in a multiscale way, by jointly utilizing node embeddings and PageRank centrality, the directions of links can be effectively inferred based on the classic gravitational heuristic formula. Extensive experimental results on four real-world benchmark directed networks validate the performance of our proposed motif-based link prediction framework. One direction of our future work is to extend the triangle motifs to square motifs, so as to capture more complex connection patterns in real-world directed networks; the other direction is to extend the current framework to solve the link prediction problem in heterogeneous directed networks.
