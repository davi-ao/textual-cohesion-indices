1. Introduction

A project is a complex network of numerous interdependent activities involved in the delivery of composite end product. The project requires a variety of input items from different suppliers to give shape to the composite end product. The specifications of these input items are dependent on project requirements, which consequently introduce dependencies among the suppliers. A project-driven supply chain (PDSC) is conceived for fulfilling the above requirements. PDSC is an integration of project activities and supply chain of the required input items (Chen & Zhao, 2008) as shown in Fig. 1. It is a combination of two disciplines supply chain and project management that requires coordination between off-site supply chain activities and on-site project activities. Vrijhoef and Koskela (2000) explored four roles of supply chain management in the project context. The first role focused on improving the interface between site activities and supply chain; the second role emphasized improvement of the supply chain, the third role focused on transferring on-site activities off-site, and the fourth role considered replacement of temporal supply chain with permanent one through postponement of customization in the initial project stages. Dainty, Millett, and Briscoe (2001) also suggested integration of suppliers into project activities to increase productivity and cost reductions. The company, which is best able to entwine its supply chain network and project network is successful in enhancing project performance and delivering the composite product (Mello and Strandhagen, 2011, Mello et al., 2017).

PDSCs differ from that of repetitive supply chain context because of following characteristics. A project allows a high degree of customization which brings uniqueness to each project’s technical, production and financial requirements. The uniqueness entails identification of new suppliers for every new project leading to discontinuous and uncertain nature of demand for the suppliers. This results in short-term buyer-supplier relationships with a deficiency in mutual trust and commitment (Khalfan et al., 2007, Laan et al., 2011, Manu et al., 2015) and thus exposes PDSCs to higher levels of design and scope changes risk, technical risk and supplier failure/default risk. Further, the interconnectedness of project network activities introduces more interdependency among suppliers. This exposes PDSCs to higher levels of communication failure and integration risks (Eriksson, 2015). Furthermore, with an upward trend of subcontracting and an emphasis on core competencies, project-oriented organizations are increasingly becoming dependent on their suppliers (Ahola, 2009). Thus, suppliers constituting the PDSC should be resilient enough in facing risks arising from the project environment.

Resilience predates to conventional risk management for dealing with the threats/risks posed by large-scale unpredictable and unforeseeable events due to a turbulent environment (Bhamra, Dani, & Burnard, 2011). Rajesh and Ravi (2015) defined resilient supplier as one who can deliver good quality products at an economical rate and is flexible enough to accommodate variability in the cost of supplies and quality parameters with shorter lead times without compromising on safety and environmental practices. The aim of introducing resilience in PDSC is to obtain a structure with preparedness to face potential risks that may impede the work in progress. Resilient PDSC evades disruptions either by avoiding the risks or transferring the risks to suppliers and capitalizing on the flexible and redundant network supply chain structures (Klibi et al., 2009). Hence decision related to the selection of resilient suppliers is crucial for the designing resilient PDSC. Researchers like Sheffi, 2005, Moore and Manring, 2009, Klibi and Martel, 2012, Sawik, 2013, Melnyk et al., 2014, Scholten and Schilder, 2015, Rajesh and Ravi, 2015, Rajesh, 2016 have investigated resilience factors either for generic or for repetitive supply chain context. However, a study that adopts the resilience perspective for supplier evaluation for PDSC context, is not present. The present study addresses this research gap. It identifies factors for evaluating the resilience of suppliers of PDSC. Further, it adopts Fuzzy linguistic guided order weighted aggregation (FLQ-OWA) approach to compute resilience scores of the suppliers. The FLQ-OWA approach is implemented in a case study of a hospital construction project. The computed suppliers’ resilience scores are used to classify suppliers in distinct categories characterized by their resilience capability Activity-wise analysis of supplier’s resilience is also performed to provide early warning signals to the project manager of contractor’s organization to work proactively with all corrective measures planned at the onset of the project.

The remainder of the paper is structured as follows. Section 2 reviews the literature on supply chain resilience. Section 3 describes the factors for PDSC supplier resilience. Section 4 presents the methodology for computation of PDSC supplier resilience. Section 5 demonstrated the proposed approach with real project case (construction of hospital/health care facility) followed by Section 6 where suppliers are analysed based on resilience score and assessed activity-wise based on their average resilience score. Based on this managerial implications and suggestions are provided. Section 7 concludes the paper.

2. Literature review on supply chain resilience

This section focuses on definitions of supply chain resilience and factors used for evaluation of resilient suppliers.

In the past, supply chain planners focused on sourcing, procuring, and logistics activities at a lower price, shorter lead times, and higher quality across multiple firms in the supply chains. Factors such as flexibility, risk management abilities, and environmental concerns were least considered (Kannan, Khodaverdi, Olfat, Jafarian, & Diabat, 2013). Recently, there is a growing body of literature on understanding the definitions, factors and evaluation criteria of the resilience of the supply chain. The term resilience is derived from the Latin word “resiliere,” which means to “bounce back” (Hosseini, Barker, & Ramirez-Marquez, 2016). This term was first popularized by Holling (1973) in his seminal work titled “Resilience and Stability of Ecological Systems.” Resilience is the property of supply chains by which they can adapt, respond, and overcome potential disruption. Christopher (2000) identified agility as one of the powerful strategies for attaining resilience. Hong and Choi (2002) suggested knowledge and understanding of supply chain structures are important for creating resilient supply chains. Rice and Caniato (2003) recommended flexibility and redundancy approaches for achieving resilience. Smith (2004) emphasized on developing an integrated environment that can update end to end interaction of orders, inventory, transportation, and distribution to maintain supply chain transparency and achieving resilience. Christopher and Lee (2004) suggested visibility and proper control as one of the powerful tools to develop resilience in supply chains. Lee (2004) suggested collaborative partnerships for effectively reducing supply chain risks. Chopra and Sodhi (2004) emphasized on adding capacity and inventory, redundant suppliers, increased responsiveness, increased flexibility, aggregate or pool demand, and increased customer accounts for developing resilience in supply chains. Tang (2006) provided nine strategies for resilient supply chains namely, postponement, strategic stocks, flexible supply base, make and buy, economic supply incentives, flexible transportation, revenue management, dynamic assortment planning, and silent product rollover. Tomlin (2006) categorized resilient strategies into two main groups, i.e., operational mitigation (strategies based on suppliers and inventory before disruption) and operational contingency (strategies based on flexibility post-disruption). Pettit, Fiksel, and Croxton (2010) developed a framework based on supply chain vulnerabilities (turbulence, deliberate threats, external pressures, resource limits, sensitivity, connectivity, and supplier/customer disruptions) and capabilities (sourcing flexibility, order fulfilment flexibility, capacity, efficiency, visibility, adaptability, anticipation, recovery, dispersion, collaboration, organization, market position, security, and financial strength) to measure the level of resilience.

Vugrin, Warren, and Ehlen (2011) considered resilient criteria into three groups, namely, absorptive capacity (geographical segregation, surplus inventory, back up supplier contracting, physical protection), adaptive capacity (rerouting), and restorative capacity (restoration budget and technical resource restoration) for evaluating suppliers. Klibi and Martel (2012) proposed following three-phase risk modeling approach for designing resilient supply chain networks against disruptions: characterization of multi-hazards and vulnerability sources; modeling of multi-hazard processes, and modeling the impact of hits on supply chain network and used simulation for generating plausible scenarios. Sawik (2013) suggested a model based on mixed integer programming for optimal selection of part suppliers and allocation of emergency parts inventory and their prepositioning among the suppliers to protect supply chain against disruption risks. Melnyk et al. (2014) opined that supply chain resilience should be considered as the heart of contemporary supply chain management thinking and presented eight strategies viz, indirect investment, discovery, information, supply chain design, buffers, operational flexibility, security, and preparedness for building a resilient supply chain. Chowdhury and Quaddus (2015) investigated eight strategies for mitigating vulnerabilities, including, backup capacity, building relation with buyers and suppliers, quality control, skill and efficiency development, ICT adoption, demand forecasting, responsiveness to customers, and security system improvements. Scholten and Schilder (2015) demonstrated the impact of collaborative activities in enhancing supply chain resilience of food processing industry. Torabi, Baghersad, and Mansouri (2015) developed a bi-objective mixed possibilistic scenario-based model to calculate the resilience of selected supply base under the effect of supply risks and disruptive events. The authors also emphasized on certain proactive strategies such as supplier’s business continuity plans, fortification of suppliers, contracting with back up suppliers to increase resilience level of the supply base.

Suppliers play a crucial role in constituting a resilient supply chain. A few studies in the literature have reported factors for evaluating suppliers. Tukamuhabwa, Stevenson, Busby, and Zorzini (2015) identified 24 resilience strategies and clustered them in the four main categories viz, flexibility, redundancy, collaboration, and agility. Rajesh and Ravi (2015) identified resilient suppliers based on five major resilient capabilities namely, primary performance factors (quality, cost and flexibility), supplier’s responsiveness (supply chain velocity and supply chain visibility), supplier’s risk reduction (vulnerability, collaboration, risk awareness, supply chain continuity management), supplier’s technical support (technological capability and research and development), supplier’s sustainability (safety and concern for environment) for an electronic supply chain. Rajesh (2016) evaluated electronics-manufacturing firm’s suppliers’ resilience based on five resilience indicators, namely flexibility, quality, responsiveness, productivity, and accessibility, using grey prediction systems.

Studies mentioned above have investigated resilience factors either for generic or for repetitive supply chain context. However, a study that adopts the resilience perspective for supplier evaluation for resilient PDSC context, is not present. The present study addresses this research gap. It first identifies the factors for evaluation of suppliers of a project and then computes resilience scores of the suppliers.

3. Factors for PDSC supplier resilience

This section describes the factors that are considered in the present study for evaluating the resilience of suppliers constituting the PDSC. The factors are identified from the extant literature and based on their relevance with respect to the PDSC context. The factors are presented in Fig. 2. Factors such as the technical capability of suppliers and flexibility are qualitative factors and are assessed based on the subjective judgement of experts. Therefore, to capture such qualitative factors fuzzy set theory (FST) is adopted. The advantage of using FST is that, it utilizes natural language to capture managerial tacit knowledge and enables the aggregation of qualitative and quantitative factors (Herrera and Martinez, 2000, Zadeh, 1965, Zadeh, 1983).

3.1. Geographic location

A supply network is defined as a configuration of suppliers employed to serve a customer base (Klibi & Martel, 2012). Bhatnagar and Sohal, 2005, Gunasekaran and Kobu, 2007 opined that the operational competitiveness of a supply chain depends on the proximity of suppliers and their markets. Proximity to markets makes the processes more responsive and ensures velocity in the delivery of products, which in turn increases the resilience of PDSCs. Thus, the higher geographical distance will lead to a lower resilience. In this study, distance (DGL) between the geographical location of the supplier and project site is expressed as a crisp number (in kilometres). The fuzzy linguistic term set corresponding to DGL on the scale of 0–1000 with cardinality 5, is shown in Fig. 3.

3.2. Variability in cost of supplies

Cost of supplies is a major determinant for assessing suppliers’ performance (Esmaeilikia et al., 2016, Rajesh, 2016). Variability in the cost of supplies (i.e., the monetary value of the items in consideration) increases the risk of project cost overrun. Once the project network is in progress, fluctuations in the cost of supplies may compound the risk of cost escalation due to dependencies between the work packages. Variations in design data, design documentation, scope changes, and information asymmetry may lead to variabilities in the cost of supplies (Koskela and Leikas, 1997, Laitinen, 1993). Hence, the higher the variability lower will be the resilience. In this study, variability in the cost of supplies (VCS) is expressed as a crisp number (in percent). The fuzzy linguistic term set corresponding to VCS on the scale of 0–100 with cardinality 5, is shown in Fig. 4.

3.3. Variability in quality parameters

Parasuraman, Zeithaml, and Berry (1985) defined quality as the degree and direction of discrepancy between client’s perception and expectations. The overall quality of supplies can be determined in terms of reliability of components, product durability, ISO certification status, product performance and conformance to standards, total quality management, rejection rate, repair and return rate and addressing feedbacks from customers. The quality is imperative for customer satisfaction in the project context. Variations in quality of items provided by suppliers will result in lower resilience. In this study, variability in quality (VQL) is expressed as a crisp number (in percent). The fuzzy linguistic term set corresponding to VQL on the scale of 0–10 with cardinality 5, is shown in Fig. 5.

3.4. Technical capability of suppliers

Hartley, Zirger, and Kamath (1997) reported that technically competent suppliers reduce supplier-related development delays (such as delays in financing) through problem avoidance and rapid problem-solving. The technical difficulty level of the project moderates the relationship between supplier quality practices and the level of supplier involvement (Primo & Amundson, 2002). The supplier is said to be technically competent if it provides error-free designs, competent enough to handle frequent changes and assure quality, and well equipped to deliver specifications on time. This will help in creating a robust network which in turn, ensures overall project resilience. Hence, technically competent suppliers are considered important for developing PDSC resilience. Technical capability of suppliers (TCS) is a qualitative factor which can be best expressed in linguistic terms. The fuzzy linguistic term set corresponding to TCS on the scale of 0–1 with cardinality 5, is shown in Fig. 6.

3.5. Variability in lead time

Lead time refers to the time within which supply chain entities deliver the order to their direct customers without any damage to the goods and services (Punniyamoorthy, Mathiyalagan, & Parthiban, 2011). Variability caused by suppliers in construction production processes decreases productivity (Tommelein, 1999), leading to scheduled delays and cost overruns (Alarcon & Ashley, 1999). Reduction of lead time variability tends to have a greater impact than the reduction of lead times, particularly when lead time variability is large (Chopra and Sodhi, 2014). Lead time variability plays a vital role in the development of PDSC resilience and thus ensures project success. Carvalho, Maleki, and Cruz-Machado (2012) considered lead time ratio as one of the factors for supplier resilience. Projects require a number of parts, components, materials from different suppliers, located at different places. Thus, variability in lead time of the suppliers associated with critical activities, can cause a delay in the project. In this study, variability in lead time (VLT) is expressed as a crisp number (in months). The fuzzy linguistic term set corresponding to VLT on the scale of 0–5 months with cardinality 5, is shown in Fig. 7.

3.6. Flexibility

Flexibility refers to the “ability of an organization to adapt to the changing requirements of its environment and stakeholders with minimum time and effort” (Erol, Sauser, & Mansouri, 2010). It can also be defined as “ability to take different positions to better respond to abnormal situations and adapt significant changes rapidly in the supply chain” (Lee, 2004). This factor is needed in all elements of the supply network and helps in the selection of risk management strategies (Datta, 2007, Zsidisin and Wagner, 2010). It has been reported that having flexible transportation systems, flexible production facilities, flexible capacity, flexible supply base, flexible labor arrangements, flexible in sourcing and order fulfilment will help the suppliers to achieve resiliency (Tang and Tomlin, 2008, Yang and Xu, 2015). This factor is assumed to be more beneficial than investing in creating redundancies (increases costs) because it not only helps the supply firms in mitigating the disruptions but also in day to day operations (Gosling et al., 2010, Sheffi, 2005). Vickery, Calantone, and Droge (1999) defined supply chain flexibility as an amalgamation of product flexibility, volume flexibility, new product flexibility, distribution flexibility, and responsiveness flexibility. In this study, flexibility (FL) is captured as a qualitative factor using linguistic terms. The fuzzy linguistic term set corresponding to FL on the scale of 0–1 with cardinality 5, is shown in Fig. 8.

3.7. Supplier and Activity-wise resilience score

The computed output using above factors i.e. supplier resilience and activity-wise supplier resilience scores will be a crisp numeric value in the range of 0–1. The fuzzy linguistic term set corresponding to computed resilience score of suppliers SRi and activity-wise supplier resilience score (ASRk) is defined on the scale of 0–1 with cardinality 5, as shown in Fig. 9. Moreover, this fuzzy linguistic term set will aid in classifying the suppliers and activities and understanding their resilience behaviour.

4. Methodology

For aggregation of aforementioned quantitative and qualitative factors ordered weighted aggregation operator (OWA) is adopted. It is a multi-criteria evaluation procedure that can be used much like fuzzy sets (Yager, 1988). It consists of two groups of weights: the criteria importance weights and order weights. The OWA procedure uses parameters that can be specified as qualitative statements in the form of fuzzy linguistic quantifiers Yager, 1988, Yager, 1996. For instance, a combination procedure may be guided by an argument that most of the criteria, at least half of the criteria, or all criteria should be satisfied. These are relative linguistic quantifiers and can be represented as fuzzy sets of a unit interval [0,1] which measure a proportion of a set. Fuzzy linguistic quantifier guided ordered weighted aggregation (FLQG-OWA) involves following: specification of the quantifier Q, generation of a set of order weights associated with Q, and computation of the overall evaluation for each alternative. 5. Case study of hospital construction project

A case study of a hospital construction project is considered. The scope of the project involved the construction of a hospital building with a capacity of 700 beds, within a timeline of 1 year and budgeted cost of 200 million INR. The project network of the hospital construction project consists of 25 activities, Fig. 12 displays the project network with precedence relationships among activities. The project starts with foundation (A1) and substructure work (A2), followed by the erection of superstructure/frame (A3). Thereafter, roof erection and waterproofing (A4) work have been carried out. After its completion, two activities namely block work (A5), and construction of sewage treatment plant (A6) having start to start precedence relationship with A4. These activities upon completion lead to electrical work (A7) and fitting of plumbing and fire-fighting systems (A8), and door and windows fittings (A9). A7 is followed by a series of activities security system installations (A10), elevators and escalators installations (A11), nurse call system fittings (A12), false ceiling (A13). A7 leads to diesel generator sets installations (A14) also as a backup during the power cut, having start to start precedence relation with it. All these activities lead to the completion of hospital automation (A19). A8 is succeeded by medical gas installations (A16) activity which is having start to start precedence relationship and three parallel activities, namely internal plastering (A17), flooring (A18), and external plastering (A18). After the completion of activities (A16) and (A17), gypstone plaster of paris (A20) is started, which is followed by internal painting (A21). The activity (A18) is followed by façade works (A22) which further leads to external painting (A23). Completion of activities of (A21) and (A23) is followed by finishing work (A24) and clearance and hand over (A25) activities.

6. Result analysis and managerial implication

The results are analysed based on supplier resilience scores and activity -wise average supplier resilience scores. Based on this managerial implications and suggestions are provided for project managers.

6.1. Assessment of suppliers’ resilience scores

Table 6 shows the input data for different suppliers involved in the project along with their resilience scores and their fuzzy linguistic term sets. The classification of suppliers based on different resilience levels is shown in Fig. 14.

Four suppliers namely S1, S2, S5, and S14 have high resilience scores. S1 has high resilience score as it has low DGL, low VCS, low VQL, high FL, and very high TCS. S2 has a high resilience score due to its low DGL and low VCS. S5 has the high resilience score due to its low DGL, very low VCS, low VQL and very high FL. S14 has high resilience score due to its very high FL, high TCS. These high resilience score suppliers expose the PDSC to minimum risk and contribute to building inherently resilient PDSC.

Maximum number (seven) of suppliers namely S4, S6, S3, S7, S9, S10 and S13 have moderate resilience scores.

Five suppliers namely S8, S11, S12, S15, and S16 have low resilience scores. S8 has low resilience due to its high DGL, very high VLT, low FL, and low TCS. S12 has low resilience score because of high DGL, high VQL and low FL. Thus, to manage S8 and S12, the project manager should either search for closely located alternative suppliers or should engage in continuous follow-ups with these suppliers to avoid delay in the delivery of items. Frequent meetings with the suppliers should be conducted to ensure proper sharing of information and knowledge regarding technical aspects and flexibility. S11 has low resilience score because it has high VCS, very low FL. S15 has low resilience score because it has very high VQL and moderate VLT. S16 has low resilience score because it has high VCS, moderate VQL and moderate VLT. Thus, to manage S11, S15, and S16 the project manager should closely monitor their production costs, quality, and delivery schedules. Due to their less preparedness to face risks, the aforementioned low resilience score suppliers pose a high threat to project performance. Their resilience scores provide early warning signals to the project managers to work proactively for adopting corrective measures at the initial stage of the project itself.

6.2. Assessment of activity-wise average suppliers resilience scores

Fig. 15 shows the resilience behavior of all activities due to different suppliers acting on it. Flooring activity A17 has low resilience due to low resilient supplier S8 providing tiles/marble/slabs. Moreover, A17 is a critical activity with zero slack. Thus, any delay in delivery of items by S8 will result in project schedule slippage. Thus, the project manager should either search for alternative suppliers who are closely located or treat the items supplied by S8 as long lead times items and place the order of these items well in advance.

Critical activities A20, A21, and A24, have low average resilience due to supplier S15, who is providing plaster of paris, paint, and primers. VCS of S15 is moderate that may cause project cost escalation. VQL of S15 is very high. The items provided by S15 determine overall finish of the hospital’s interior and play a major role in customer experience and satisfaction. Thus, the project manager should adopt close continuous monitoring mechanisms with frequent site visits for controlling production costs and quality issues on items provided by S15. As the supplier is technically competent and has the flexibility to incorporate customer specifications, the project manager can deploy a technical expert to work closely with the supplier which will reduce variability in cost and improve quality of items delivered.

Critical activities A1 and A5 have high resilience whereas A7 and A8 have moderate average resilience. Due to highly resilient suppliers S1 and S2 and moderately resilient supplier S3 provide items to activity A1 were involved. Thus, to manage the critical activity A1, the project manager should address moderate DGL of S3 by either searching for alternative suppliers who are closely located or include a penalty clause for late delivery in the contract with S3 to lower the risk of delayed delivery due to low flexibility. Moderately resilient suppliers S4 and S6 provide items to A5 and A7 respectively. S4 and S6 have moderate VLT thus the project manager should engage in frequent follow-ups with these suppliers to avoid any delay of the critical activities. S7 who provides fire extinguishers, sensors, smoke detectors, ducts to activity A8 has very high VQL and very low TCS. Since A8 is a critical activity, the project manager should ensure timely delivery of items by ordering items well in advance by clearly stating technical specifications of the safety items and ensure stringent quality adherence by stating issue of quality standards certificate by the auditing bodies as the scope of deliverables in the contract with the supplier.

Critical activities A3 and A4 have high resilience due to highly resilient suppliers S1 and S5, and S2 respectively. Thus, the project manager does not need to invest resources for increasing resilience of these suppliers. However, because the activities lie on the critical path, periodic monitoring is essential to avoid potential risks.

Non-critical activity A2, A16, and A18 have high resilience due to highly resilient suppliers S1 and S2. The moderately resilient supplier S3 provides miscellaneous materials like sand, stone chips to A2. The consumption quantity of these miscellaneous materials is highly variable due to rework, wastage and pilferage. Due to very low FL, S3 will not be able to respond to changes in the volume of the demand of these materials. Therefore, the project manager should order large quantities by including sufficient safety stock.

Moderately resilient supplier S7 and highly resilient supplier S14 provide pipes and medical gas cylinders respectively to non-critical moderately resilient activity A19. The installation of the medical gas pipeline system and gas cylinders in health care facility require trained personnel for its operation because these gases are utilized for patient care by clinical staff. Therefore, the medical gas suppliers need to be certified and possess required technical skills in providing medical supplies. The project manager should check the quality certification of S14 before placing the order and deploy quality check measures when the items are delivered.

Low resilient supplier S11 and S16 provide items to non-critical activities A10 and A15 respectively. Activities A10 and A15 are low in resilience as S11 and S16 have high VCS thus, a price fluctuation clause should be added in the contract for managing the project cost escalation due to fluctuations in the price.

Low resilient supplier S12 is responsible for providing escalators and lifts to non-critical activity A11. A11 is low in resilience as S12 has high DGL, therefore, the project manager should treat these items as long lead time items and order them in advance. The TCS of the supplier is high, but it has high VQL; thus, the project manager should transfer the risk of low quality to the supplier by including free after sales service and warranty clauses in the contract.

The above analysis of resilience scores of suppliers and activity-wise resilience scores provides comprehensive, information related to PDSC suppliers to improve their performance. Thus, using the performance scorecards of the suppliers, a balance between operational objectives and internal/external risks can be controlled by identifying their role associated with the activities. Moreover, as per the project requirement, project managers can trade-off between the factors well in advance. A consistent evaluation of resilience scores of all suppliers in this manner will transform the supplier relationship management system into a more predictive rather than a reactive one.

7. Conclusion

PDSCs are basically an integration of two different disciplines: project management and supply chain management. This integration or collaboration is termed so because supply chains in PDSCs are project-driven. In other words, supply chains are unique and non-repeatable and are established only for fulfilling the requirements of a single project. PDSCs provide a comprehensive, proactive approach to mitigate risks associated with volatile supply chains dedicated to the timely delivery of complex projects. In complex projects, such as make-to-order and EPC, there is heavy dependence/reliance on suppliers for raw materials, finished parts, and components. These supplier dependencies add significant risks to project success. In the current study, an attempt was made to identify factors imparting resilience capabilities to suppliers in a real project case (hospital building) and these capabilities were analysed quantitatively using the FLQ-OWA technique in the context of PDSCs. Based on the evaluation, the suppliers are segregated into three categories, viz, highly resilient (S14, S1, S5, and S2); medium resilient (S3, S4, S6, S7, S9, S10, and S13) and low resilient (S8, S11, S12, S15, and S16). Furthermore, the results of activity wise analysis indicated that the suppliers feeding critical activities are low in resilience due to high geographical distances, it is suggested to use other modes of transportation (airfreight) to avoid supplies delay. If the supplier is technically less competent, it would be advisable to deploy a small contractor’s team at supplier site to overcome variations and revisions in required specifications. For handling quality related issues, it is suggested that project team representatives visit supplier's site and carry out stringent quality checks not only at the end of product delivery but also in intermediate (processing and product development) stages. For suppliers associated with non-critical activities and suppliers with high variations in the price of supplies and geographical distances, a penalty clause must be included in the contract to cope with these issues. Frequent meetings must be conducted to ensure sharing of information and knowledge regarding technical aspects with the suppliers for overcoming technical incompetence. To avoid lead time variability, third-party logistics providers can be involved. For handling quality issues, frequent visits to the supplier site are advised. These results would help project managers compare suppliers and take steps to improve the resilience scores of suppliers. Also, avoiding operational risks and disruptions risks.
