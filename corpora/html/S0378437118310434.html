1. Introduction

There are many kinds of complex networks in the real world, such as social networks [1], [2], [3], [4], protein–protein interactive networks [5], transportation networks [6] and the World Wide Web networks [7]. Detecting underlying communities from complex networks is beneficial for understanding their characteristics and functions. Many community detection algorithms have already been applied in such applications as customer recommendation systems [8], online marketing [9], critical node identification [10] and reality epidemic spreading [11].

A community can be defined as a group that interacts with each other more frequently than with those outside the group. Based on the general description, a great many community detection algorithms have been proposed in the last few decades. For instance, spectral optimization based methods [12], modularity optimization based methods [13], subspace decomposition based methods [14], neighborhood based methods [15], [16], maximal sub-graph based methods [17], [18], core–vertex based methods [19], and so on. However, most of these methods, which only consider network topology structure information, are only suitable for networks with a distinct community structure, but not for those with a vague community structure. The performance of these algorithms will be greatly reduced if they are applied to extremely sparse networks or attribute networks. Whereas community detection in static networks usually focuses on one-mode networks [17], [18], [19] and bipartite networks [20], [21], [22], in this paper we study attribute networks which belong to one-mode networks. Attribute networks refer to those in which each node or edge in the network has its own attributes or contents. Take Twitter, for example, where each user has attributes such as their followers, the tweets they publish and their interests. We should not just focus on the network topologies when detecting communities for two reasons. One reason is that the available structure information is insufficient because most of complex networks are extremely sparse in the real world. Sometimes two nodes both belong to the same community even if there is no edge between them. For instance, one scientist in the scientific collaboration networks may only have contact with several other experts he knows, while the community he belongs to may contain many more scientists with whom he is not familiar. Another reason is that nodes do not only have structure information but also semantic information, i.e., their attributes or content. This underutilized semantic information, which is related to the community detection task, is helpful for improving the quality of community detection.

Therefore, integrating attribute information is very necessary for community detection. There are many traditional methods using node content only, such as K-means [23], LDA [24] and N-cut [25]. These algorithms are widely used in image segmentation, information retrieval and text clustering. However, they ignore the rich information encoded in the linkage of the network. Hence, lots of community detection algorithms combining network structure with content are proposed [26], [27], [28], [29], [30]. Pei et al. [26] employ a nonnegative matrix tri-factorization based community detection framework with three types of graph regularization to combine the relations with content seamlessly. The Non-Negative Multiple Matrix Factorization model [27] utilizes complementary data as auxiliary matrices so that the sparseness of data is improved by decomposing the target and auxiliary matrices simultaneously. Qi et al. [28] study community detection combining with edge content in social media networks. Wang et al. [29] study the problems of community detection and inference of their semantics simultaneously by integrating network topology and semantic information of nodes. Yang et al. [30] propose Communities from Edge Structure and Node Attributes (CESNA) model which statistically models the interaction between the network structure and the node attributes. Though node content can indeed provide more abundant information, it always mixes with some irrelevant attributes and noisy data. These noisy attributes may not work on improving algorithm performance and may even make the results worse. Therefore, we can utilize the accurate individual labels obtained from background knowledge or additional information to ameliorate this situation. The individual labels include many aspects involving the network structure or semantic information. For example, we can easily know whether the research field of an author is data mining or not according to his personal profile in the scientific collaboration networks. This kind of semi-supervised information is beneficial to our clustering objects because we can achieve higher accuracy and robustness in the processing of community detection by using the individual labels.

Recently, some work using semi-supervised community detection algorithms has attracted a surge of interest. According to the form of semi-supervised constraint, most semi-supervised community detection algorithms can be divided into two categories, i.e., algorithms based on pairwise constraints and algorithms based on individual labels. The pairwise constraints can be depicted by the must-link and cannot-link constraints. For example, Yang et al. [31] propose a general, high-speed and effective semi-supervised community detection framework by transforming the original network into an equivalent but much smaller Super-Network which ensures the pairwise constraints. Ver Steeg et al. [32] study the impact of pairwise constraints on the accuracy of community detection from the perspective of statistic mechanics. Yang et al. [33] discover and explore the importance of cannot-link constraint in community detection via Multi-variance Mixed Gaussian Generative Model. Ma et al. [34] integrate the must-link and cannot-link constraints into the adjacency matrix and decompose it to get the community preference matrix. Yang et al. [35] propose a novel nonlinear reconstruction method by adopting deep neural networks for representation and extend the method to a semi-supervised community detection algorithm. Different from pairwise constraints, the individual labels use the true community assignments of nodes. The individual labels are harder constraints than the pairwise relationships and they can be turned into pairwise relationships, not vice versa. For instance, Liu et al. [36] examine the semi-supervised community detection problem based on discrete potential theory, using combinational Dirichlet to incorporate individual labels and the network topology. Liu et al. [37], [38] study the community detection problem based on label propagation, which can propagate the labels from the labeled nodes to the whole network nodes. There are still many other semi-supervised community detection methods like [39]. Although the above mentioned semi-supervised methods can achieve good performance, most of them can only utilize partial information of the network so that they cannot describe the network more comprehensively. Furthermore, a large amount of semantic information which involves all nodes is ubiquitous but is not utilized effectively. Therefore, the performance of community detection can be further improved if we integrate the information more comprehensively. Since node attributes and individual labels influence and promote each other synergistically, it is vital to study how to effectively integrate them.

In this paper, to alleviate the aforementioned issues, we put forward a non-negative matrix factorization (NMF) based framework which can effectively combine with the network topology, node attributes and individual labels. Inspired by the sparseness of general complex networks and the existence of rich attribute information in nodes, we integrate the node attributes into the network structure to enrich the available information which is valuable for community detection. Furthermore, many real complex networks have a fuzzy community structure, so we utilize the individual labels of nodes to improve the clearness of the community structure. The semi-supervised information is as important as the attribute information since they mutually reinforce and promote each other. On one hand, the individual labels of nodes belong to strong supervision while the attributes of nodes belong to weak supervision, so the amount of available information is relatively small if only using strong supervision and there will be noisy and irrelevant information if only using weak supervision. On the other hand, the individual labels can be seen as another kind of structural information which is stronger than topology, so they are complementary to the node attributes or content. In this way, we can obtain more accurate community detection results. Specifically, two NMF components are utilized to effectively catch the relationship between the network topology and community structure, the relationship between the communities obtained from the perspective of structure and the communities obtained from the perspective of attributes respectively. Meanwhile, serving the community labels of nodes as the semi-supervised constraint, we minimize the difference between the community labels obtained by our method and the community labels obtained from the ground truth. The multiplication update rules which guarantee that the result obtained is the optimal solution are derived for the objective function. While extensive experiments have been conducted on real-world data sets, our algorithm is superior to several other state-of-the-art algorithms in terms of accuracy and robustness. And the capability of semantic analysis enhances the interpretability of communities. The results also show different types of information are effectively integrated and all of them can enhance the performance of community detection.

The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 describes basic definitions and notations presented in the paper. Section 3 presents our semi-supervised community detection algorithm in detail, including the proposed model, the derivation of update rules and the analysis of time complexity. Section 4 shows the experiment results and detailed analysis on real-world network. Section 5 concludes the whole paper.

2. Preliminaries

The network can be modeled as a graph, i.e., G=(V,E), in which V denotes the set of nodes and E denotes the set of edges. For simplicity, the type of graph in the paper is assumed as an unweighted and undirected graph. Before introducing the algorithms in detail, we will first explain some notations that appeared in the paper. As shown in Table 1, n represents the number of nodes, m is the number of node attributes or community attributes and k denotes the number of communities. A is the binary matrix to denote the relationship between each two nodes, i.e., A(i,j)=1 denotes there is a link between node i and node j,A(i,j)=0 otherwise. Z is the clustering indicator matrix to denote the relationship between nodes and communities, i.e., Z(i,j)=1 denotes node i belongs to community j and Z(i,j)=0 otherwise. Let us denote the node attribute matrix and the community attribute matrix as N and H respectively. Both of them are also binary matrices, and N(i,j)=1 denotes node i has the attribute j and H(j,i)=1 denotes community i has the attribute j. It is worth noting that the number of node attributes and community attributes are the same. Let us denote the node label matrix as Zr, we set Zr(i,j)=1 if node i belongs to the community j in reality and Zr(i,j)=0 otherwise. It should be noted that the sum of each row is equal to 1 because each node only belongs to one community in reality. S denotes the node selection matrix, which is a diagonal matrix. Note that the term containing matrix S does not have a typical balancing parameter that assigns its weight because such weighting is carried out by S.S(i,i)=γ denotes that node i has been selected, i.e., the individual label of node i is known and S(i,i)=0 otherwise. The parameter γ controls how strongly the supervision is imposed. With the symbol definitions mentioned above, we formally describe the problem of community detection in the paper in Table 1.

3. Proposed Method

In this section, we first present the proposed semi-supervised community detection framework integrating the network structure, attribute information and individual labels. Then the update rules which can solve the optimization problem are presented, as well as the pseudocode of our algorithm. Finally, the complexity analysis is given.

3.1. Overview

Fig. 1 presents the community detection process of the general NMF model which utilizes network topology only, while Fig. 2 shows the community detection process of our method. Assuming the network has five communities, we take nodes 3 and 5 as examples. The adjacency matrix is obtained by utilizing the topology structure. Meanwhile, if the individual labels of nodes 3 and 5 are known and they both belong to the second community, then the third and fifth rows of the node label matrix should be both [0,1,0,0,0]. When minimizing the difference between the clustering indicator matrix Z and the node label matrix Zr, the values of the third and fifth rows in the clustering indicator matrix Z will be as close as possible to the corresponding values in matrix Zr so that these two nodes can be sorted into the same community in all probability. On the other hand, we consider the attribute information of nodes and communities. If the attributes of nodes 3 and 5 are both similar to the attributes of community 2, then the third and fifth rows in the product of the node attribute matrix N and the community attribute matrix H will both have a stronger preference for community 2. Thus when minimizing the difference between the clustering indicator matrix Z and the product of N and H, we will prefer to sort nodes 3 and 5 into the same community. Meanwhile, it is proved that the individual labels and the attribute information are mutually reinforced under the NMF model in the experimental part. They are effectively integrated together and both of them can improve the original community detection effect.

Topologies based community detection component. The traditional NMF model only utilizes the topological structure of the network. In general, the relation between node i and node j is modeled by Aij=∑p=1kZip⋅ZpjT. In other words, the probability of node i connecting to node j is seen as the sum of the probability that these two nodes have connections in each community. When modeling the connection relationships of all nodes, we use ‖A−ZZT‖F2 to measure the difference between the real topology structure and the modeled topology structure by using the clustering indicator matrix, where ‖⋅‖F denotes the Frobenius norm of a matrix. In fact, the square of the Frobenius norm is equivalent to the square loss function and is a common way to quantify the quality of the factorization result. Therefore, the community detection component which only considers the network topology is as follows: (1)minZ≥0‖A−ZZT‖F2.

Attributes based community detection component. Due to the sparsity of most of complex network and the existence of abundant attributes or content of nodes, we integrate the attributes of nodes into our framework so that the quality of community detection can be further improved. Take nodes i and j as example, we utilize Zij=∑p=1mNip⋅Hpj to model the relationship between two kinds of communities, i.e., we use the community obtained from attributes to represent the community obtained from structure. In other words, this formula means one node is likely to belong to a community if the attributes of the node are very similar to the attributes of the community. Meanwhile, we utilize ∑i=1k‖H(:,i)‖12 to obtain a sparse solution of the community attribute matrix. H(:,i) denotes the column i of H,‖⋅‖12 denotes the mixed-norm regularization, i.e., l1,2-norm. Usually, we can get the sparse solution by using the l1 regularization term so that the attributes of communities will be more distinguishing [40]. At the same time, adding the l2 norm can ensure the values of community attributes reasonable. Therefore, the effect of this term is to make the attributes of each community more sparse so that their distinctions are more obvious and the attribute information more meaningful at the same time. If the difference between different communities is clearer, the nodes can be distributed into the correct community more easily. In a word, the attributes based community detection component can be written as: (2)minZ≥0,H≥0‖Z−NH‖F2+β∑i=1k‖H(:,i)‖12.

Individual labels based community detection component. Considering that the existence of noisy attributes and fuzzy communities in the network, the individual labels are used to make up these deficiencies and the community structure can be clearer. We use ‖S(Z−Zr)‖F2 to measure the differences between the real community labels and the calculated community labels of the selected nodes. The node selection matrix S is used for choosing which individual labels can be seen as known labels and determining the weight proportion of the supervision. The purpose of this term is to make the computed community labels of these selected nodes as close to the real community labels as possible. The community detection component based on individual labels is as follows: (3)minZ≥0‖S(Z−Zr)‖F2.

The unified framework. By combining the community detection components in (1), (2), (3), the problem of semi-supervised community detection in attribute networks has the following objective function: minZ≥0,H≥0‖Z−NH‖F2+α‖A−ZZT‖F2+‖S(Z−Zr)‖F2+β∑i=1k‖H(:,i)‖12,where α,β are the parameters to control the weight proportion among the components of network topology, attribute information and sparsity constraint on community attributes.

3.2. Optimization

The objective function of the proposed algorithm is minZ≥0,H≥0O(Z,H)=‖Z−NH‖F2+α‖A−ZZT‖F2+‖S(Z−Zr)‖F2+β∑i=1k‖H(:,i)‖12.

We can find that it is not jointly convex with respect to Z and H, such that traditional convex programming solutions cannot solve our optimization problem. Although it is unrealistic to get the global minimum of the objective function, the local minimum is obtained by using the iterative multiplicative update rules. First we update Z with the fixed H and then update H with the fixed Z. This process is repeated until the objective function converges to a stable value. We can guarantee the objective function will not increase after each iteration.

3.2.1. Update Z with H fixed

When we update Z with H fixed, the optimization problem becomes the following formula: minZ≥0O(Z)=‖Z−NH‖F2+α‖A−ZZT‖F2+‖S(Z−Zr)‖F2.

The Lagrange multiplier method is utilized to handle the constrained optimization problem. Thus by adding the Lagrange multiplier to the objective function, the equivalent function is obtained: O(Z)=tr(ZZT−2ZTNH+HTNTNH)+αtr(AA−2AZZT+ZZTZZT)+tr(ZZTSS−2ZTSSZr+ZrTSSZr)+tr(ΘZT).

Then setting the partial derivatives of O with respect to Z equal to 0 ∂O(Z)∂Z=2Z−2NH−4αAZ+4αZZTZ+2SSZ−2SSZr+Θ=0.The representation of the Lagrange multiplier is derived as: Θ=−2Z+2NH+4αAZ−4αZZTZ−2SSZ+2SSZr.The final solution of Z should satisfy the KKT conditions, i.e., ΘijZij=(−2Z+2NH+4αAZ−4αZZTZ−2SSZ+2SSZr)ijZij=0.The update rule can be derived as follows: (4)Zij←Zij(NH+2αAZ+SSZr)ij(Z+2αZZTZ+SSZ)ij14.

3.2.2. Update H with Z fixed

When we update H with Z fixed, the optimization problem becomes the following formula: minH≥0O(H)=‖Z−NH‖F2+β∑i=1k‖H(:,i)‖12.This formula is equal to the following optimization problem [41]: minH≥0O(H)=‖(Nβe1×m)H−(Z01×k)‖F2,where e1×m is a row vector in which elements are equal to one and 01×k is a zero vector.

According to [42], we obtain the following update rule: (5)Hij←Hij(N′TZ′)ij(N′TN′H)ij,where N′=(Nβe1×m),Z′=(Z01×k). The proof of convergence is also in [42].

3.3. Complexity Analysis

Most adjacency matrix A and node attribute matrix N of real complex networks are sparse matrices. In addition, the node selection matrix S in this paper is the diagonal matrix. In our semi-supervised algorithm, the main computation cost is in the calculation of the update rules (4) and (5). To facilitate understanding, we assume that the time complexity of the multiplication of two matrices, e.g., a n×m matrix and a m×k matrix, is O(nmk). Therefore, the time complexity of our optimization algorithm is O(r(n2k+mnk)), where r is the number of iterations and m,n,k represent the number of attributes, nodes and communities, respectively, as shown in Table 1. Furthermore, there have been many parallel algorithms on NMF recently proposed [43], [44], so our proposed framework may have the potential to be applied to large-scale networks.

4.2. Performance metrics

To evaluate the quality of the communities detected by the different methods, we adopt the accuracy (AC) and the normalized mutual information (NMI) to measure the clustering performance [45]. These two metrics are both constructed by comparing the labels obtained by our approaches and the labels provided by the data sets. The accuracy (AC) can measure the percentage of correct labels obtained by our algorithms. Given a data set which contains n objects, let ci be the community labels of object i obtained by different approaches, and let ri be the real labels of object i provided by the data sets. Then the accuracy (AC) is defined as follows: AC=∑i=1nδ(ri,map(ci))n,where δ(i,j) is the delta function that equals 1 if i=j and equals 0 otherwise.map(ci) is the mapping function that maps the clustering label ci to the equivalent label provided by the data sets. The best mapping can be obtained by using the Kuhn–Munkres algorithm [46].

Another metric normalized mutual information (NMI) is often used to measure the difference between the true partition of the network and the clustering results obtained from our algorithms. Given two different clustering sets A and B, the NMI is defined as follows: NMI=−2∑i=1CA∑j=1CBCij⋅logCij⋅NCi⋅⋅C⋅j∑i=1CACi⋅⋅logCi⋅N+∑j=1CBC⋅j⋅logC⋅jN,where CA(CB) is the number of communities in partition A(B), N is the number of nodes, C is a mixed matrix in which the element Cij represents the number of nodes belonging to community i in partition A and also belonging to community j in partition B at the same time. Ci⋅ is the sum of row i in matrix C and it represents the number of nodes selected arbitrarily from the data sets belonging to community i in partition A. Similarly, C⋅j is the sum of column j in matrix C and it represents the number of nodes belonging to the community j in partition B. The higher the value of NMI, the more similar the two partitions are. The NMI value ranges from 0 to 1. When the value of NMI is 1, it indicates that partition A and partition B are identical, and a 0 value of NMI indicates that the two partitions are entirely different.

4.3. Baselines

For simplicity, the algorithm proposed in this paper is called SCDAN (Semi-supervised Community Detection in Attribute Networks). In order to demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed method, we compare SCDAN with other competitive community detection algorithms. These algorithms can be categorized as the link-only methods, the content-only methods, the methods combining link and content and the methods combining link and prior, respectively. These baselines are introduced as follows:

Link-only method: This group of community detection algorithms only utilizes the topology structure of the network and ignores the node attribute values. We choose SNMF and SBM as the representative algorithms of this category [47], [48]. SNMF is also a kind of matrix factorization method and is fit for undirected networks because it decomposes the adjacency matrix into two identical matrices. SBM belongs to the algorithms of stochastic blockmodels, but unlike other blockmodels that ignore variation in vertex degree, it is a degree-corrected stochastic blockmodel and thus is suitable for applications in real-world networks.

Content-only method: These methods only consider object attributes and they ignore the network structure. We choose CAN and SMR as representatives of this category [49], [50]. CAN is a clustering model which can learn the data similarity matrix and community structure simultaneously, enabling it to obtain the optimal solution. SMR belongs to the algorithms of subspace clustering and it introduces the enforced grouping effect conditions and a new affinity measure based on the grouping effect, which proves to be more effective than the commonly used one.

Combination of link and content: This kind of approach uses node content along with the links. In this category, we consider PCL-DC and SCI [29], [51]. PCL-DC is a discriminative model for combining the link and content analysis, and it considers additional factors that could affect the community memberships and isolate the contents that are irrelevant to community memberships. SCI is a novel nonnegative matrix factorization model with two sets of parameters, the community membership matrix and community attribute matrix. The use of node attributes improves upon community detection and provides a semantic interpretation to the resultant network communities.

Combination of link and prior: These methods are semi-supervised community detection algorithms which utilize structural connections and individual labels. We consider NMF_LSE [52] and the variant of SCDAN which is called SCDAN_NA in this group. NMF_LSE implicitly encodes the must-link and cannot-link constraints by modifying the adjacency matrix of the network. SCDAN_NA only utilizes the topology information and the individual labels of nodes, i.e. the second and third items in the objective function. The derivation of update rules is similar to our algorithms.

4.4. Experimental Results

4.4.1. The quality of community detection

In the specific experiments, we set the number of communities to be the same as the actual number according to the ground truth. If the number of communities is unknown in advance, we can determine it by model selection. There are many model selection criteria for us to choose, some of which are based on approximated likelihood or Bayesian inference [53], [54] and some of which [55], [56] focus on network cross-validation which is usually done through a node splitting procedure. In practice, we can choose an approximate criterion from the criteria mentioned above.

During each experiment, we set the maximum iteration to be 3000 times and run 10 repetitions on every occasion. As for the parameter setting, we set α=40,β=3 for the Cornell and Washington data sets; we set α=30,β=30 for the Texas and Wisconsin data sets and α=1,β=1 for the Cora and Citeseer data sets. Meanwhile, the nonzero value of the node selection matrix S is set to be 1 million after parameter tuning so that the supervision can work well. As for more complex networks, we can set different values in practice.

The accuracy and NMI for different methods on six data sets (WebKB can be seen as four separate data sets) are shown in Table 3, Table 4. The experimental result achieving the highest accuracy or NMI is highlighted respectively in each column. The proportion of individual labels in semi-supervised algorithms is thirty percent. We draw the following conclusions from the results shown in the tables.

The overall performance of the proposed SCDAN algorithm is best, because it can achieve the highest accuracy and NMI in most data sets. Table 3 shows the accuracy results of different methods. For instance, our proposed method can reach about 11% to 47% improvement compared to the competitors on the Cornell data set, about 25% to 52% improvement on the Washington data set, about 22% to 45% improvement on the Wisconsin data set, about 8% to 33% improvement on the Texas data set and so on. Table 4 presents the NMI results of different methods. For example, our approach achieves about 21% to 34% improvement compared with the other methods on the Cornell data set, about 33% to 52% improvement on the Washington data set, about 27% to 41% improvement on the Wisconsin data set, about 8% to 25% improvement on the Texas data set and so on. Thus, it is obvious that our algorithm performs far better than any other competitors in the first four data sets. Even if our algorithm comes in slightly below NMF_LSE in the Citeseer or Cora data sets, it is still much more competitive compared to most methods in the Citeseer and Cora data sets. The reason for this may be that the Cora and Citeseer data sets have some noisy attributes and the community detection result can be worsened by utilizing the attribute information. Therefore, the methods using node content are only relatively inferior. But in general, our algorithm SCDAN which utilizes the network topology, node content and individual labels simultaneously achieves the best results compared to the other four types of algorithms.

When we theoretically analyze these comparison algorithms, it is not difficult to come to similar conclusions. On one hand, the link-only methods, i.e., SNMF and SBM, both regard only the adjacency matrix obtained from the network topology as known information. Whereas many real-world networks are sparse networks, this type of information is not very effective when the community structures of the networks are fuzzy. On the other hand, CAN and SMR which belong to the content-only methods both use only the data matrix obtained from node attributes for community detection. But there will be many irrelevant or inaccurate attributes in the data matrix, so that the performance of community detection will be affected if the quality of node attributes is not high. To avoid these problems, our proposed methods integrate the topological information and node content by using the non-negative matrix factorization model in order to complement and promote each other.

The semi-supervised algorithms perform well, while NMF_LSE modifies the adjacency matrix by utilizing the prior information and takes it as input, so NMF_LSE just takes the prior information as the pre-processing problem of community detection and cannot describe the natural properties of the original semi-supervised problem. Our method integrates the individual labels into the framework of community detection so that they can play a greater role in the process of community detection. Another semi-supervised approach SCDAN_NA, i.e., a variant of SCDAN, only uses a small amount of individual labels for community detection leaving a mass of ubiquitous and valuable attribute information underutilized. Our method avoids these problems by integrating the network topology, node content and individual labels into the community detection framework, thereby achieving a more satisfactory result compared to the competitors.

An interesting observation is that these comparison algorithms have significant differences for different data sets, which indicates sensitivity to data sets among these competitors. However, our algorithm can always produce the satisfactory result on different data sets, which shows that SCDAN possesses strong robustness and steadiness, an important and valuable characteristic for real complex networks in our life.

It is worth noting that our method is easily extended to the detection of overlapping communities. When we assign community labels of nodes according to the clustering indicator matrix Z, we specify if Zijin matrix Zis above a certain threshold, then node i is regarded as belonging to community j. By using this extension method, we can naturally obtain the overlapping community structures. The measurement of performance on overlapping communities can refer to [57], [58].

4.4.2. Priors proportion impact

As shown in Fig. 3, we plot the broken line graph of the NMI for five different algorithms with different individual label proportion on four real data sets. These comparison algorithms are SCDAN, SCDAN_NA, NMF_LSE, SCI and the common NMF methods, while the other competitors are omitted because the results can be found in Table 3 or Table 4 and all of these methods without individual labels will be horizontal lines in the figure. In the figure, the difference of these algorithms is that SCDAN utilizes three components, i.e. topology structure, attribute information and individual labels; NMF_LSE and SCDAN_NA use the structure information and individual labels; SCI uses the network topology and node attributes and NMF only utilizes the topology structure of the network.

In this way, we can clearly see the function of node attributes and individual labels. It can be concluded that the NMF method has the worst performance compared to the other four methods according to these figures. The SCI, NMF_LSE and SCDAN_NA algorithms all perform better than NMF, so it is obvious that the attribute information and individual labels are beneficial for improving the performance in community detection. Meanwhile, the SCDAN algorithm can achieve the best results among all the methods, a result which demonstrates that the node attributes and individual labels enhance and promote each other. It can also be observed that the performance of the semi-supervised community detection algorithm NMF_LSE is no match for ours under the supervision information of any proportion. In addition, in the SCDAN, SCDAN_NA and NMF_LSE methods, the value of NMI gradually becomes higher and higher with the proportion of the individual labels increasing in general. This shows that the more individual labels obtained in advance, the better the community detection result will be, a phenomenon that also matches our intuition.

Fig. 4 shows the changing curve of the accuracy for five different algorithms with the increment of the individual label proportion on six data sets. Similarly, our algorithm SCDAN performs best among all methods because it can achieve the highest results with different individual label proportion on different data sets in general. The larger the proportion on individual labels, the higher the accuracy we can obtain. But the NMF_LSE is slightly different, such that the proportion of individual labels has no obvious effect on the accuracy of the WebKB data set. The reason may be that NMF_LSE is sensitive to different data sets and performs poorly on small data sets.

4.4.3. Parameter sensitivity analysis

The sensitivity analysis of parameter α and parameter β in the objective function is shown in Fig. 5. We set parameter α varying from 1 to 100 and the step size as 10. Similarly, parameter β varies from 1 to 10 and the step size is 1. According to the heat map, the variation tendency of the accuracy and NMI with different parameter values on the Washington data set is clear at a glance. The redder the color, the higher accuracy or NMI it can achieve. It can be seen that SCDAN is highly sensitive to parameter β, and the data set prefers to set parameter β into three. Therefore, the proportion of the constraint on the node attributes is very important for our algorithm. As for parameter α, the difference among different values of α is not obvious, since it has slightly different results only when the value of parameter α is relatively small.

4.4.4. Case study

To illustrate the superiority of the proposed method, we conducted a case study to visually show the results of community detection. Word clouds of the detected community attributes in the Last.fm data set are shown in Fig. 6. The URL of the data set is http://ir.ii.uam.es/hetrec2011/datasets.html. This data set was obtained from the Last.fm online music system, whose users are interconnected in a social network generated from Last.fm “friend” relations. These relations can be seen as the topological information of the social network. Each user has a list of their most listened to music artists and tag assignments, with 1892 users and 11,946 tags in the Last.fm data set, so that each user has 11,946 attributes when forming the node attribute matrix. Considering that the Last.fm data set does not have the ground truth of the community labels, we did not conduct quantitative experiments on this data set as before. Instead, we used the Louvain method to set the number of community to 32 and obtain the community labels as the ground truth in our algorithm [59]. Because some differences exist between the real community labels and the labels obtained from the Louvain method, we set the coefficient of the node selection matrix S to 1. Meanwhile, by carrying out parameter sensitivity analysis, we set parameter α and β to 1 as well. With four communities taken as examples, every community displays ten attributes which are most relevant to itself. The font size of the attribute is proportional to the relevance to the community, the larger the font size of the attribute, the more relevant it is to the community.

Fig. 6(a) features heavy metal bands. For example, “Metallica”, “Queensryche”, “Sound Garden”, “Backyard Babies”, “Guns n’ Roses” and “Dream Theater” are all heavy metal groups. “Glam metal”, “heavy metal” and “hard rock” all belong to this music genre. Fig. 6(b) is mainly about the rock band “Duran Duran” and rock music, including the music genres of “new romantic”, “synth-rock” and “new wave”. A “supergroup” is usually used to describe a successful or well-known rock group. As for the community in Fig. 6(c), its attributes all belong to electronic music genres, such as “hardstep”, “darkstep”, “IDM”, “EDM”, “industrial”, “drum and bass”, “dark ambient” and “ambient”. “Electronic” and “electro” have the most direct relationship to electronic music. The forms of these music influence each other and mix together, so they have some similarities. For example, “hardstep” is also a subgenre of “durm and bass” and “idm” originally grew out of “ambient” music. Fig. 5(d) is mainly about life and democracy. Subjects like “life of pain”, “female empowerment”, “sickness” and “the cure”, are matters of concern to the average person. It is worth mentioning that “Deutsche Welle” is Germany’s public international broadcaster, which features stories about liberty and democracy. According to the above analysis, it can be seen that every community has unique and distinct attributes and we can obtain better understanding of these communities by utilizing their semantic information.

5. Conclusions

In this paper, we have presented a novel semi-supervised community detection method SCDAN, which integrates network topology, node attributes and individual labels into the non-negative matrix factorization model for community detection. SCDAN can formulate these components as a unified problem and balance their contributions in a seamless way. Extensive experiments illustrate the effectiveness and robustness of our proposed method. In the future, we plan to exploit more types of priors from different sources such as the must-link set or cannot-link set. Finding a way to speed up our algorithms for more efficient performance on large-scale networks is another research priority.
