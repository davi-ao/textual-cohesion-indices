1. Introduction

In decades, adopting an Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) system has been a promised way for corporation to gain competition advantages in the world [10], [14], [15]. However, the performance of an ERP system is not easily identified. This is due to the volatility of the internal factors and the external environment. On the other hand, the major obstacle is the lack of an effective and quantitative evaluation method developed. So, many corporations had invested large amount of capital to the ERP systems but have no easy way to evaluate their performance.

Even though, many researchers have contributed qualitative methods to evaluate the performance of ERP systems. These researches can be classified in two general parts. One part is to investigate the financial performance when corporation invested an ERP system. For example, Wu et al. [17] proposed a method to justify the tangible and intangible performance of an ERP system. The tangible aspect was analyzed by Hochstrasser model [17], and the intangible one was engaged by fuzzy evaluation approach which combined nine value drives in corporation value creation processes. The other part puts the emphasis on the manipulation of critical factors/items that can be obtained from various ways. The first way is by literature review, such as the work of Dowlatshahi [8] who surveyed current ERP literatures and identified the four ERP strategic factors (cost of ERP implementation, implementation time and return on investment issues, ERP employee training, and effective use of ERP features/applications), and the work of Al-Mashari et al. [1] who concluded a taxonomy of the critical success key factors (SKF) involving technical and organizational imperatives. The second way is from questionnaire, such as those done by Lin et al. [12] who used the data envelopment analysis approach to evaluate the relationship between ERP continuous investment and technical efficiency. They also utilized the Tobit regression to investigate the relationship between efficiency scores and the ERP continuous investment based on the concept of total cost ownership. The third way are from heuristics such as the work of Chand et al. [4] who provided a balanced-scorecard based framework for valuing the strategic performance of an ERP system, the work of Yang et al. [19] who presented a performance-evaluation model of ERP implementation utilizing fuzzy measures, the work of Chang et al. [5] who constructed a conceptual model to evaluate the performance and competitive advantages associated with ERP from a supply chain management perspective, and the work of Lin et al. [13] who proposed a statistical method based on Parasuraman and a revised performance evaluation matrix to set up a standard performance upper and lower control limits in terms of Taguchi method and Shewhart control charts.

For quantitative method, Chen and Lin [6], [7] firstly proposed novel analytic methods for evaluating the realtime performance of ERP systems. These models can be used to analyze/evaluate the performance of a general ERP system. They established a theoretical basis for the performance evaluation of ERP systems. According to the approaches, researchers may conduct analysis and investigation against an ERP system. The brief of the approach is that: assuming the performance of an ERP system is closely related with the end-users, i.e., if the end-users are not familiar with the system, the performance of the underlined system will be low, but if the end-users are knowledgeable about the system, the performance of the underlined system will be good. Therefore, based on this knowledge, the performance of an ERP system can be analyzed and evaluated after effectively assessing the capability of the end-users about the underlined system.

The proposed approach considers that an ERP system is a complex network composed of various business processes. It can be called an ERP net. The nodes of the net are the persons responsible for the operations of the processes. The links are the precedence relationships between operations. The net can activated by various business requests from source node of a process. Then, the documents (jobs) are generated and sent to its succeeding nodes for further operations. Thus, the performance of an ERP system is closely related with the document flow in the net. The performance failure of an ERP system is therefore defined by the condition that the document flow of the system is under the acceptable level d. An acceptable level of an ERP system is a minimum requirement of the throughput of the system defined by the manager to identify the current condition of the system. To address the capacity of a node, it is said that a low capacity node can typically be assessed by the lack of right knowledge about the operations of the underlined system. Some researches had revealed the fact that the knowledge of end-users about ERP operations is an important success factor in the ERP systems [2], [8], [20]. To validate the ERP knowledge captured by the users, an objective examination/assessment is valuable. Generally, the results of the test are given into grades such as “excellent”, “good”, “normal”, “bad” or “failed” instead of the exact scores. These linguistic grades can be advantageously handled by the fuzzy set theory [11], [16]. To enhance the realtime-evaluation capability of an ERP system and because Chen and Lin’s approach did not cover the link failure cases, this paper extends their approach to cover the link failure situations. A link failure in an ERP net means that the software or hardware between processes may malfunction. This is a usual phenomenon in practice. For example, such failures can be identified by alert lights such as “green”, “yellow” or “red” corresponding to “normal condition”, “minor damaged” or “seriously damaged” respectively. Thus, based on the nodes and links real time conditions, a fuzzy linguistic performance index Rd∼ for an ERP system can be created by defuzzifying the probability Rd of the maximal document flow not less than d.

The probability calculation is based on minimum paths (MPs) method [6], [7]. An MP is a sequence of nodes and links from source node to sink node without cycles. The remainder of the work is described as follows: The assumption for the ERP systems discussed here is presented in Section 2. The net model of an ERP system is discussed in Section 3. A procedure to evaluate Rd∼ is proposed subsequently in Section 4. Then, the calculation of the linguistic performance index is illustrated by some numerical examples in Section 5. Finally, Section 6 concludes this paper.

2. Assumptions

Let G=(A,N,M) be an ERP net where A={ai|1⩽i⩽n} is the set of links, N={ai|n+1⩽i⩽n+s} is the set of nodes, and M=(m1,m2,…,mn+s) is a vector with mi (an integer) being the maximal capacity of component (node or link) ai. Such a G is assumed to satisfy the following assumptions.

1.

The capacity of each component ai is an integer-valued random variable which takes value from the set {0,1,2,…,mi} according to a given distribution governed by μi, where μi is a membership function mapping from fuzzy set {excellent, good, normal, bad, failed} or {green, yellow, red} corresponding to nodes or links respectively to real interval [0,1].

2.

Flow in G satisfies the flow-conservation law [9].

3.

The components are statistically independent from each other.

The capacity of a component is the throughput to successfully process the documents (jobs). Since the different components represent different objects in the process, the respective probability distributions are also different from each other. The probability is governed by μi membership function. For certain conditions, one can adopt the same set of membership functions for all objects to fit in this approach. For example, all individuals in this process possess the same level of skills, then, to simplify the statistic observation, they can adopt the same set of membership functions. Table 1 shows two alert lights of μ1 membership functions for link a1. If link a1 gets a “red” light in the ERP assessment, the membership degree (probability) of capacity 5 for link a1 is μ1(red)(5)=0.005, but if it gets a “green” light in the assessment, the probability of capacity 5 for link a1 is μ1(green)(5)=0.210. This means that the probability of the capacity greater or equal to 5 for the link a1 being green is larger than that for the link being red in the assessment. Normally, the probability in Table 1 is obtained by an empirical observation on a process during a time frame or moving time frame. For example, supposing the system status is on green light (normal condition). We take a one-month observation of a1, and get the statistics (counts per half day) in Table 2.

3. The ERP net model

In the context of an ERP environment, a process initiate a document flow from the source node through a set of alternative paths depending on the business considerations without loop (i.e. through MPs) to the destination node in order to complete the business process. Suppose mp1,mp2,…,mpz are totally the MPs from source node to sink node. Thus, the ERP net model can be described in terms of two vectors: the capacity vector X=(x1,x2,…,xn+s) and the flow vector F=(f1,f2,…,fz), where xi denotes the current capacity of component ai and fj denotes the current flow on mpj. Then, such a vector F is feasible if and only if(1)∑j=1z{fj|ai∈mpj}⩽miforeachi=1,2,…,n+s.

Eq. (1) describes that the total flow through ai cannot exceed the maximal capacity of ai. We denote such set of F as UM≡{F|F is feasible under M}. Similarly, F is feasible under X=(x1,x2,…,xn+s) if and only if(2)∑j=1z{fj|ai∈mpj}⩽xiforeachi=1,2,…,n+s.

For clarity, let UX={F|F be feasible under X}. The maximal flow under X is defined by V(X)≡max∑j=1zfj|F∈UX.

3.1. System performance evaluation

Given the level d (the required document flow), the system performance Rd is the probability that the maximal flow is not less than d, i.e., Rd≡Pr{X|V(X)⩾d}. To calculate Rd, it is advantageously to find the minimal capacity vector in the set {X|V(X)⩾d}. A minimal capacity vector X is said a lower boundary point for d if and only if (i) V(X)⩾d and (ii) V(Y)<d for any other vector Y such that Y<X, in which Y⩽X if and only if yj⩽xj for each j=1,2,…,n+s, and Y<X if and only if Y⩽X and yj<xj for at least one j. Suppose there are totally t lower boundary points for d:X1,X2,…,Xt, the probability Rd is Pr⋃i=1t{X|X⩾Xi}. Let μR be the membership function mapping from fuzzy set {acceptable, re-certified, declined} to [0,1]. Then, the linguistic performance index Rd∼ is defuzzified from μR-1(Rd), where μR-1 is a reverse function of μR.

3.2. Generation of all lower boundary points for d

At first, we find the flow vector F∈UM such that the total flow of F equals d. It is defined by the following demand constraint.(3)∑j=1zfj=d.

Then, let F={F|F∈UM and satisfies Eq. (3)}. We show that a lower boundary point X for d exists, if there is an F∈F such that the following lemma holds.

Lemma 3.1

Let X be a lower boundary point for d, then, there is an F∈F such that(4)xi=∑j=1z{fj|ai∈mpj}foreachi=1,2,…,n+s.

Proof

Let X be a lower boundary point for d, and there is an F such that F∈UX and F∈F. It is known that ∑j=1z{fj|ai∈mpj}⩽xi∀i. Suppose there is a k such that xk>∑j=1z{fj|ak∈mpj}. Set Y=(y1,y2,…,yk-1,yk,yk+1,…,yn+s)=(x1,x2,…,xk-1,xk-1,xk+1,…,xn+s). Hence Y<X and F∈UY (since ∑j=1z{fj|ai∈mpj}⩽yi∀i), which indicates that V(Y)⩾d but contradicts to that X is a lower boundary point for d. Thus xi=∑j=1z{fj|ai∈mpj}∀i.  □

Given any F∈F, we generate a capacity vector XF=(x1,x2,…,xn+s) via Eq. (4). Then the set Ω={XF|F∈F} is built. Let Ωmin={X|X is a minimal vector in Ω}. Lemma 3.1 implies that the set Ω includes all lower boundary points for d. The following lemma further proves that Ωmin is the set of lower boundary points for d.

Lemma 3.2

Ωmin is the set of lower boundary points for d.

Proof

Firstly, suppose X∈Ωmin (note that V(X)⩾d) but it is not a lower boundary point for d. Then, there is a lower boundary point Y for d such that Y<X, which implies Y∈Ω but contradicts to that X∈Ωmin. Hence, X is a lower boundary point for d. Conversely, suppose X is a lower boundary point for d (note that X∈Ω) but X∉Ωmin i.e., there is a Y∈Ω such that Y<X. Then, V(Y)⩾d which contradicts to that X is a lower boundary point for d. Hence, X∈Ωmin.  □

We propose a procedure in Section 4 to illustrate the generation of linguistic performance index covering link failures.

4. Solution procedure

Fig. 1 denotes the proposed procedure for applications. Firstly, the net model for the ERP system is created. The lower boundary points for the ERP net are generated by the algorithm discussed in SubSection 4.1. Meanwhile, the user training and training examination can be took place. Along with the link conditions by the link assessment, the lower boundary points and the grades of user examination enable the calculation of the linguistic performance index Rd∼, which is discussed in SubSection 4.2.

4.2. The generation of Rd∼

From Ωmin, we get the set of lower boundary points. Suppose there are totally t lower boundary points for d, say X1,X2,…,Xt. The probability Rd for these lower boundary points is Rd=Pr{⋃i=1t{X|X⩾Xi}}. Let Bi={X|X⩾Xi}. Then Rd is rewritten as(5)Rd=Pr⋃i=1tBi.

To obtain Pr{Bi}, let’s suppose the result of ERP test or link assessment for the rth component is ηr and the rth column of the minimal vector in Bi is lir, we get Pr{Bi}=∏r=1n+s∑j=lirmrμr(ηr)(j) by definition. Next, we find the respective probabilities of membership function for the corresponding capacities of these components. Rd=Pr⋃i=1tBi is then derived by applying the inclusion–exclusion rule. Finally, Rd∼ is defuzzified from Eq. (6), where μR-1 is a reverse membership function of Rd∼.(6)Rd∼=μR-1(Rd).

4.3. Storage complexity

The number of solutions for F is bounded by Cz-1z+d-1. The number of XF generated is then bounded by Cz-1z+d-1. The storage space needed for Ωmin is then bounded by OCz-1z+d-1 in the worst case. In sum, the total storage space needed is OCz-1z+d-1 in the worst case.

4.4. Computation complexity

A pairwise comparison is required for generating Ωmin between Cz-1z+d-1 solutions. This takes O(n+s)2Cz-1z+d-1 time to generate Ωmin. In short, the total computational time required is O(n+s)2Cz-1z+d-1 in the worst case.

6. Conclusion

Chen and Lin’s novel analytic models [6], [7] established a theoretical basis for the performance evaluation of ERP systems. These models allow us to analyze/evaluate the realtime performance of a general ERP system. According to their approaches, researchers may conduct several interesting analyses and investigations on an ERP system. However, their models did not cover the link failure cases. A link failure in an ERP system means that the software or hardware between processes may malfunction. This is a usual phenomenon in practice. This paper extends their approach to cover the link failure cases. For example, such failures can be identified by alert lights such as “green”, “yellow” or “red” corresponding to “normal condition”, “minor damaged” or “seriously damaged” respectively. Thus, based on the nodes and links real time conditions, a fuzzy linguistic performance index Rd∼ for an ERP system can be created by defuzzifying the probability Rd of the maximal document flow not less than d.

In practical application, a clearly meaningful performance index is more advantageous than that of the traditional scales. Especially, when the index is objectively obtained from the analytical model, the conviction of such index is obvious. This paper presents a detailed numerical example to explain the proposed approach. By the illustration of several scenarios, the proposed approach is valuable for a company to assess its ERP performance from time to time.
