1. Introduction

The theory of complex networks emerged in the process of development of graph theory. First of all, the theory is associated with solving tasks related to networks in the case when analytical graph approaches can no longer be used (dimension and complexity of computation, nonlinearity of the structure changes in time). There are numbers of graph structures can be considered as complex networks, including the simplest, in which a connection between nodes do not have certain weight. Nodes and connections correspond to certain numerical values.

Directed networks with weighted connections are used for resolving wide variety of problems. For example, the Lancaster’s law of combat, proposed in 1916, describes the interaction of the two warring parties as a simple network of two nodes and two links between them [1]. Later on this model was generalized for the case of a large number of parties involved in a conflict [2]. Modifications of Lancaster’s model require higher complexity network approach, for example, guerrilla warfare model, described in work [3]. Another problem of the theory of complex networks lies in controlling of linear and nonlinear systems. An example of a problem is the task of controlling collective behavior using networks with directed weight links [4]. We can also consider the well-known problem of PageRank computing [5], [6] as well as HITS (Hyperlink induced topic search) index [5], [7], [8] as the ranks of complex networks (for example the Internet). Complex networks are also applied for text analysis [9], which can be applied to identify fake and software-generated papers. Investigation of a system, not only as individual components but as a whole, can be successfully applied to a biology system, considered as a biological network [10]. The approach can be used for detection of collaboration and citation networks [11], [12], explaining their dynamics, information mining in a consumer community network [13], [14]. Short texts can be analyzed using the methods and concepts of complex networks as well (language networks) [15]. And, finally, we should also mention usage of cognitive maps [16], [17]. A cognitive map is a directed graph with given weights of connections [18]. According to given rules (which can be different for different statements of the problem), it is necessary to find the weights of the nodes and the strength of the mutual (often indirect) influence of the nodes.

It is necessary to clearly understand that the definition, setting of the task in the analysis of the network structure depends not only on the structure of the network, but on “rules of the game” of this network as well. Steady weights of nodes/links are calculated using given rules (or algorithms). In fact, these algorithms are completely different for different tasks.

The research of the mentioned (and many other) problems within the framework of the theory of complex networks can be conditionally divided into two parts. The first of them is formal investigation of a given network, for example to establish conditions for the existence of a solution (fixed point), its stability, etc [6], [17].

The second part of the problem is less formalized and consists of three points, each of which is a significant problem itself. The first point is to create a complex network and declare initial values of nodes and connections, the second one is to declare the “rules of the game” - an algorithm that corresponds to the task within the task, allows calculating the weights of nodes in the PageRank task. And the third point is the interpretation of the obtained results, namely the weights of nodes or links. The first and third problems relate to the understanding of the real world’s subject areas, the rules of functioning of network models, and their interpretations completely depend on experts in given areas.

After the network is defined, the initial values of the weights of the links and/or nodes are defined and “rules of the game” are set. This results in formalizing of the task. And the only remaining task is to find the final values of the weights of the nodes (or links).

2. Problem statement

In this part, we consider such processes in which some nodes (which correspond to the concept of the real world) can affect others by changing their numerical values. The magnitudes of the influence are determined by present weights of directed bonds, which can be either positive or negative.

Let us consider several problems related to the mutual influence of concept nodes on such graphs. The simplest problem is shown in Fig. 1a, where we have two concept nodes in the network, with the first node affecting the second node and the second node affecting the first node. The numerical value of the magnitude of the influence is denoted by ε. If the value of the 1st concept 1 is given and the magnitude of its influence on concept 2 is given as well, then it is natural to assume: φ2=φ1+ε, that is (1)φ2−φ1=ε.

Other simple networks are shown in Fig. 1b, c, d. In the network depicted above (Fig. 1b) there is no direct influence of concept 1 on concept 3, thereby we can denote ε13=0. However, there is an indirect effect of the 1-factor on the 3-factor by the concept 2 (ε12≠0,ε23≠0). Let us determine the full impact of one concept on the other in the absence of a direct link. Thus, we need to determine influence, which includes not only direct (in the case of their existence), but also every indirect influence. It is supposed that if one can find a path (via an oriented graph) from one concept (for example α), to another (for example β), then there is an influence (an indirect one) of the concept α on the concept β. Let us denote the total influence, covering influence over all possible paths, as Kαβ. On the other hand, it is necessary to find the corresponding K-matrix, with elements of Kij, thereby to describe the function K=Kε. Elements of the matrix of ε define direct influences between concepts. Elements Kαβ of matrix K set every influence, including indirect one. And, surely, in the general case inequality Kαβ≠εαβ applies (for example, for the cognitive map shown in Fig. 1b ε13=0, while K13≠0). Accordingly, the networks that correspond to the matrices of contiguity εik and the matrix K=Kε are different networks.

Let us determine the requirements that the function Kε should satisfy:

1. Linearity, Kc⋅ε=c⋅Kε, c = constant. In particular, it does not matter in which units the influence εαβ is given.

2. For any given matrix ε matrix K can be found.

3. For finite values of elements of matrix ε, the elements of matrix K must be finite.

4. The result of the calculation must be mathematically stable. Small deviations of ε should lead to small deviations of K. This means that if Kε+δε=Kε+δKε and δε∕ε≪1, then δK∕K≪1.

The motivation of our work is the need for calculations the mutual influences of nodes in various networks, in particular, in the so-called “cognitive maps”. Cognitive maps are network structures (directed graphs), the nodes of which are domain objects, concepts and edges are causal relationships or “influence” connections (in the general case, it is assumed that such connections can be bidirectional), to which both, positive and negative values can be attributed (regardless of the direction of the edges). Application of the method of calculating the mutual influences of nodes through enumeration of all possible paths leads to a problem of high complexity, which still requires additional restrictions (for example, maximum number of possible rounds of cycles). There are also other methods, approximate ones, which do not require complete enumeration of all cyclic paths in the graph, for example, the impulse method or the method of fuzzy cognitive maps. The disadvantages of the impulse method include its non-scalability, i.e. in some cases, changes in the scale of the values of all edges leads to a disproportionate change in the level of mutual influence of nodes and, in some cases, to a divergent series obtained in the process of calculation according to this algorithm. As for the method of fuzzy cognitive maps, its main disadvantage is that when calculating the mutual influences of nodes, the order of values of influences between individual nodes is preserved, however, absolute values of these influences are leveled due to the use of smoothing functions like hyperbolic tangent. The method proposed in our paper is devoid of the indicated disadvantages; it is rather simple algorithmically and its complexity is a priori lower than that of the method of complete enumeration with cycles.

3. Method of calculating the K-matrix

The main idea of the proposed method is to use the analogy of calculating the influence in an electrical circuit’s network. With this analogy in mind, it is assumed that the direct power εik of the ith concept’s influence on the concept k is an analog of the electromotive force (EMF) in an electric circuit ik. In this case φk−φi is an analog of the difference of electric potentials between the corresponding nodes. Then Kαβ is the difference of potentials φα−φβ. The resistance of a subcircuit of the network is assumed to be equal to 1. Such an analogy leads to the fact that the calculation K=Kε must be equivalent to solving the Kirchhoff equations for linear electrical circuit.

Unfortunately, in the proposed case, such a simple and transparent analogy cannot be realized. The point is that the considered networks are digraphs. As for “electric” analogy, electric circuits contain diodes that transmit currents and charges in only one direction.

This means that for the network shown in Fig. 1a in accordance to formula (1) relation φ2−φ1=ε applies, in the case when ε<0, φ2−φ1=0. Electrical current in a closed electric circuit appears when electromotive force direction is the same as the current of the diode direction. Difference of potentials appears in the case of opened electric circuit. In the opposite case (electromotive force direction is opposite to the current of the diode) current does not appear. Thus, the sign of ε specifies “positivity” or “negativity” of an influence, which may contradict a given direction of influence. This does not allow us to consider negative influences.

In order to avoid this contradiction, the following method is proposed. The method allows calculating the measured influence of one concept on another (Fig. 2) (for example to determine the influence, of a concept α on a concept β in a given network (Fig. 2a)):

1. There are all possible paths without using a loop from node α to node β (Fig. 2b). At the same time, in each of the paths, a link can enter any node no more than once.

2. All the paths obtained are connected to a single network in the way that all paths start from one node α and terminate at the node β (Fig. 2c).

3. In the obtained graph (which, strictly speaking, is not a subgraph of the network under consideration), the direction of the links is ignored. The resulting graph is called the Kαβ-graph. We note at once that in this case different graphs correspond to different pairs of indices.

4. The numerical value of the influence of concept α on concept β, which is denoted by the same symbol Kαβ, is calculated according to Kirchhoff’s rules (now for the network, obtained at step 3, which does not contain non-linear elements; thus the solution always exists and is unique).

Fig. 3. demonstrates a network in the general case (for an arbitrary network) obtained for selected nodes α and β that are connected by M parallel paths, each of which consists of Nm consecutive links m=1,2,…,M. Accordingly nm, this is the nth bond in the mth path, containing the EMF, equal to εmnm.

The desired value of the influence of concept α on concept β, which is defined as the electric potential difference: Kαβ=φβ−φα, is calculated in accordance with Kirchhoff’s rules: (2)Kαβ=∑m=1Mεm∕Nm∑m=1M1∕Nm,εm=∑nm=1Nmεm(k),where εm is the total EMF in the mth path, Nm- the number of connections along this path.

5. Nodes ranking. Pressure and influence

In accordance to (3), the K-method makes it possible to calculate the mutual influence of one concept (α) on another concept (β), and consider the influence of all nodes on each other. We can calculate such characteristics of the cognitive map as “paired influences”.

In addition to this “paired influence” based on the K-matrix, we can introduce the notion of “collective interaction” and calculate it. Let us consider two above-mentioned characteristics, namely “pressure” - ψ and “influence” - ν, (Fig. 4). The arrows representing relationships on the diagrams do not correspond to the influences on the cognitive map, however, they indicate the existence of the corresponding component of K-matrix.

The components of the vectors ψ and ν are calculated on the basis of the matrix as follows: (4)ψβ=∑αKαβ,να=∑βKαβ.

Since these cognitive maps do not take into account the influence of the node on itself, the elements with α=β do not enter into expressions (4).

According to definition (4), pressure describes the total impact of all other concepts on concept β, and influence να is the sum of the effects of concept α on all other concepts.

In addition to the definition (4), the amplitude of pressure aψβ and amplitude of influence aνα are introduced, their absolute values can be calculated as follows: (5)aψβ=∑α|Kαβ|,aνα=∑β|Kαβ|.

Thus, for calculation of aνα, the sign of the influence of the concept α on concept β does not matter, only its value is taken into account (see Table 1).

To explain in details, how the K-method works in comparison to the impulse method, let us consider two simple cognitive maps. The first of them, which we denote as Q (Fig. 8a), consists of two concepts and one connection, and the second one (denoted as S) consists of two concepts and two connections - (Fig. 8b).

In cognitive map Q, the weight of a connection, specifying the influence of node 1 on node 2, is equal to Q21; node 2 does not affect node 1, Q21=0.

In a cognitive map S, the weight of the connection that determines the influence of node 1 on node 2 is equal to S12, and influence of node 2 on node 1 is equal to S21

Calculation of the matrix K for a cognitive map Q is trivial, in this matrix with dimension 2 over 2 all the elements (except for the element K12=Q12) are equal to zero.

In the case of a cognitive map s, when calculating the influence of node 1 on node 2 from the graph S (Fig. 8b-I) it is necessary to select the subgraph (Fig. 8b-II), from which relation K12=Q21 immediately follows. Similarly, during calculation the influence of node 2 on node 1 (Fig. 8b-iii) relation K12=Q21 applies.

Cognitive map s (Fig. 8b) was analyzed using an iterative method for several cases [16].

In the first case, it is assumed that S12=S21 = 1/2 ; and if we assume that viinit=0 and P (0) = 1, then ν1=ν2=2. If we apply K-method under S12=S21 = 1/2, then the influence of node 1 on node 2 and node 2 upon node 1 are equal to K12=K21 = 1/2. The second case slightly differs from the first: both values S12 and S21 are 4 times higher: S12=S21=2. Such a calculation shows that in this case mutual influence of nodes increases by the same amount, namely ν1=ν2=8, K12=K21=2. Thereby, both methods the impulse and K-method, are consistent with each other.

The following two cases considered in [16] refer to the situation when the weights have different signs, for example S12 = 1/2 and S21 = 1/2. In this case the application of the K-method gives us K12 = −1/2 and K21 = −1/2, thereby the influence of nodes is identical in absolute value, and opposite in sign, which corresponds to qualitative considerations. The impulse method at S12 = −S21 = 1/2 gives different signs of ν 1 and ν 2 although their modules are different - ν1=0.8, ν2=0.4, which is very strange, considering presence of the symmetry in the problem. The most dramatic difference between the K-method and the impulse is observed if the weights of the bonds in the previous example are increased 4 times (S12 = −S12 = 2).

In this case the series calculated in the impulse method diverges, and at n=100 the values of |ν1| and |ν2| reach high value equal to ∼1030. K-method under 4-times’ increase of Sik times leads to values of Kik4 times higher. Note, that in the impulse method application, the ratio ν 1 (n)/ν 2 (n) remains finite while n increases, and is a periodic function with a period n = 2, that successively assumes the values 3, −1/3, 3, …

Despite some similarity of the results of node ranking, obtained using the K-method and the impulse method, it should be noted that the impulse method does not satisfy the requirements formulated above. In particular, when the adjacency matrix is changed by the same value, the ranking of the coefficients (nodes) can be changed. And in some cases this leads to the fact that calculations by the impulse method generally cease to converge.

Conclusions

The article suggests new pair characteristics of complex network nodes and the method of their calculation (K-method), which can be used to calculate the mutual influence of concepts in cognitive maps. Due to simplifications in the proposed method, it becomes possible to use the simplified calculation mechanism, similar to one, applied in electrical engineering. Two new weight characteristics of the concept nodes are proposed, namely pressure and influence, which reflect the network structure and “collective interaction”.

It should be noted that the proposed algorithm is devoid of the main problems and contradictions inherent in the widely used impulse method [16], [19], namely:

1. Divergence of the values of the connection weights when the series of degrees of the adjacency matrix Wk diverges.

2. The increase in the elements of the adjacency matrix W by the same amount not only changes the magnitude of the components of the resulting vector, but also changes their order in the ranking.
