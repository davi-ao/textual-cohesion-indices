1. Introduction

As soon as the concept of software ecosystem was first proposed in 2003 by Messerschmitt and Szyperski [1], it has aroused great interest in both academia [2], [3] and industry (Apple, Microsoft, SAP, etc.).

The main reason for the rapid development of software ecosystems lies in their economic, strategic, and technical advantages [4]. As the highest level of current software engineering, software ecosystem has high reliability and scalability, and can achieve rapid evolution and adjustment to the changing market needs [5]. Through the openness of the system, software enterprises can speed up the emergence of innovative achievements and share the development cost with partners, so as to improve the efficiency of software development and reduce the operating cost.

However, software ecosystem is still a new research topic, and the technology that sustains their success and growth is much less understood [6], [7]. The structural characteristics of software ecosystem are helpful to reveal the hidden rules and some behavior characteristics of the system, improve its viability and the recycling of knowledge, so as to ensure the stability and robustness of the system. How to mine the structural characteristics of software ecosystem is a subject worth studying.

In essence, software ecosystem can be described as a complex network. Community structures are critical towards understanding not only the network topology but also how the network functions [8]. Therefore, community detection has become a hot issue of complex network in the past decades. For software ecosystem, community detection can help us master its behavior, evolution and development, which is of great theoretical and practical significance [9]. Franco-Bedoya et al. have also stressed that the next challenge of software ecosystem becomes the relationship with all the parties in their ecosystem [10]. Community structure is an important reflect of individual relationship.

Since Girvan and Newman put forward the concept of community detection in 2002 [11], various community detection algorithms have been proposed, mainly including graph segmentation method [12], cluster-based method [13] and heuristic method [14].

The existing community detection methods of complex network are mainly based on the topology structure. However, the dependence and interaction between different individuals of software ecosystems are very complex, and the scale of data access is far larger than that of traditional software system. In order to fulfill reasonable community division, the network structure and other information of software ecosystem must be clearly described and analyzed. The existing community detection methods, due to the lack of full consideration of various characteristics, are difficult to obtain satisfactory community division in software ecosystem.

In view of this, this paper presents a method of community detection by comprehensively evaluating developer cooperation intensity. First, we combine network topology information and developer interaction information in software ecosystem to calculate the developer cooperation intensity, so as to deeply explore the relationship between developers from both topological and semantic properties. Then a community detection algorithm, Algorithm Based on Developer Cooperation Intensity (ABDCI), is proposed by referring to the hierarchical clustering idea of Louvain algorithm. Finally, the proposed method is applied to a typical open source software ecosystem, GitHub. The experimental results show that the proposed method can identify clear community structure for the developer collaboration network in GitHub.

The structure of this paper is as follows. Section 2 presents the related work. We introduce the relevant concepts and our research object in Section 3. Section 4 gives the measurement of the cooperation intensity between developers. A new community detection algorithm (ABDCI) is proposed based on the idea of hierarchical clustering. We apply the proposed method to a typical open source community in the software ecosystem, GitHub in Section 5. Section 6 presents possible threats. Finally, conclusions are given in Section 7.

2. Related work

Since the paper studies community detection for software ecosystem, this section first summarizes the works on software ecosystem, and then reviews the methods of community detection.

2.1. Software ecosystem

The term business ecosystem was first coined by Moore [15], in which interactions among participants are performed around a shared technological platform. Software ecosystem is essentially a special manifestation of business ecosystem in software industry.

The definition of software ecosystem given by Messerschmitt and Szyperski [1] is: “a software ecosystem refers to a collection of software products that have some given degree of symbiotic relationships”. Jansen et al. [16] have defined a software ecosystem as: “a set of actors functioning as a unit and interacting with a shared market for software and services, together with the relationships among them”. Another definition by Bosch [5] is: “the set of software solutions that enable, support and automate the activities and transactions by the actors in the associated social or business ecosystem and the organizations that provide these solutions”. The diversity of relationships among participants supports the software ecosystem health and enables the creation of innovative business models [17].

As soon as the concept of software ecosystem is proposed, it has been widely concerned by the academia and industry. Through the openness of the system, software enterprises can speed up the emergence of innovative achievements and share the development cost with partners, so as to improve the efficiency of software development. As a result, more and more enterprises have begun to use software ecosystem platforms to build software, such as Apple’s IOS and Google’s Android Smartphone ecosystem [18], and Eclipse and FOSS’s open-source ecosystem [19].

As a complex system, the reliability and robustness of software ecosystem are concerned by different ecosystem participants: software developers, end-users, investors, etc. Jansen [2] proposed a maturity model of software ecosystem governance, through which stakeholders can make better decisions on whether to invest in an ecosystem. Based on the document metadata of R software package, Plakidas et al. [20] generated a variety of evaluation indicators to measure the performance of R ecosystem. Franco-Bedoya et al. [10] applied automatic and manual searches on different sources and used a rigorous method to retrieve the selected papers. The results showed that existing research on several topics related to open source software ecosystem is still scarce. Scacchi and Alspaugh [21] identified the challenges and opportunities for improving the security of software ecosystems and supply chain processes.

In addition to theoretical research, people also pay attention to the empirical analysis of software ecosystem. Dittrich [22] provided a holistic understanding of the observed and reported practices to device specific support for the development in software ecosystems. Tripathi et al. [23] aimed to identify the effect of the six ecosystem elements (entrepreneurs, technology, market, support factors, finance, and human capital) on minimum viable product development by empirical study. Berger et al. [4] analyzed five ecosystems, ranging from the Linux kernel through Eclipse to Android. Christensen et al. [24] performed a descriptive, revelatory case study of the Danish telemedicine ecosystem and experimentally designed, implemented, and evaluated the architecture of 4S. Wnuk et al. [25] conducted an interview-based case study of an ecosystem around Axis network video surveillance systems, interviewing 10 internal experts and 8 external representatives of 6 companies, complemented by document studies at Axis.

It should be noted that although the research on software ecosystem has made some progress, the existing achievements are still preliminary and one-sided, and software ecosystem is still a new research field. On the other hand, the great value of software ecosystem in the development of disciplines and engineering applications is a consensus. This shows that it is urgent to study the targeted theory and method to provide technical support for the healthy development of software ecosystem.

In essence, software ecosystem is a huge complex network, and community structure is one of the most important topological characteristics of complex network. In view of this, this paper intends to divide the community organizations of software ecosystem, which is of great significance to reveal the functions of the system, discover the hidden rules of the system, predict the behavior of the system, and the evolution and development of the system.

2.2. Community detection

Since Girvan and Newman put forward the concept of community detection in 2002 [11], various community detection algorithms have been proposed, mainly including graph segmentation method [12], cluster-based method [13] and heuristic method [14].

Graph segmentation method divides the network into several subgraphs so that the number of connected edges between subgraphs is the smallest. The most famous graph segmentation method is the Girvan–Newman (GN) algorithm proposed by Girvan and Newman [26].

Cluster-based method mainly clusters nodes according to some metrics that describe the structure of the network community, among which the most commonly used is the modularity [27].

Newman first proposed a community detection algorithm (Fast Newman) based on modularity optimization [28]. Heuristic method considers the community detection as an optimization problem, and uses heuristic algorithms to solve it, such as genetic algorithm [29], particle swarm algorithm [30], simulated annealing algorithm [31].

Although the community detection methods of networks has made a lot of results, the research on the community detection of software ecosystem is still rare. However, people have done a lot of research on the community attribute of software ecosystem.

Toral et al. analyzed the behavior of virtual communities for Open Source Software (OSS) projects. Stolee et al. [32] aimd to gain a better understanding of end-user programmer communities, the characteristics of artifacts in community repositories, and how authors evolve over time. Grossman et al. [33] made an investigation into the adoption and use of the Unified Modeling Language in the software development community. Toral et al. [34] analyzed the behavior of virtual communities for Open Source Software (OSS) projects.

The community detection of software ecosystem can reveal the deep dynamic structure of the system, which is of great theoretical and practical significance for us to master the structure and behavior of the system, as well as the evolution and development of the system [9]. At present, there is little work in this area. This paper studies the community detection of software ecosystem, which is very necessary.

3. Preliminaries

There are different types of relationships among developers in a software ecosystem, such as attention, submission of common repositories, comment interaction. Submission is a behavior that can best reflect the contribution of participants to the repository. Developers who submit products to the same repository always have similar skills and preference, and should be classified into the same cluster. So this paper build the software ecosystem network taking the joint repository submission as the cooperation relationship.

The software ecosystem network is defined as a graph G=(V,E), where V={v1 ,v2 ,…,vn} is the vertex set, which corresponds to the set all developers; E={eij} is the edge set, which reflects the cooperative relationships between developers. eij=1 if and only if developers vi and vj have at least one shared repository, otherwise, eij=0.

The community partition of network G is to decompose it into several subgraphs G1,G2,…,Gn, where Gi=(Vi,Ei), satisfying Vi⊆V, Ei⊆E. A ideal community division requires that there are more edges between the vertices in each community subgraph, and as few edges as possible between the vertices in different communities.

Traditional community detection algorithms model the network as either weighted or unweighted. In the software ecosystem, considering the difference of closeness degrees between developers, we weight each edge using cooperation times, which means the number of times two developers cooperate. Let W={wij|eij∈E} be the set of edge weights, where wij represents the cooperation times between two developers vi and vj.

Although cooperation times can distinguish the cooperation intensity between developers to some extent, it is not enough to reflect the complex relationship between developers in the software ecosystem with huge amount of information and interaction.

In this paper, we use cooperation times as the weight of edge, and on this basis, measure the cooperation intensities between different developers from two aspects, i.e. topology and semantic properties. Similarity cluster-based community detection algorithm considers that the greater the similarity between nodes, the higher the possibility that they belong to the same community. Therefore, the key of the algorithm is to reasonably define the cooperation intensity between developers that will be spread in next section.

4. Developer cooperation intensity

In traditional complex networks, the similarity between nodes is mainly calculated based on the topological properties. To actual networks, such as software ecosystem, in addition to the network topology, we need to consider the impact of other information when measure the similarity of vertices. On one hand, the relationship between developers is directly reflected by their cooperation behavior, which can be calculated by the connectivity (i.e. topology properties) in the developer cooperation network. On the other hand, if we want to further consider the possibility of cooperation, we need to measure the impact of cooperation behavior on the relationship between developers, that is, we need to consider the degree of their interaction in cooperative repositories, which can be quantified by semantic properties. Therefore, this section explores the potential relationship between developers from two aspects, i.e. topology properties and semantic properties, base on which the cooperation intensity between developers is calculated.

4.1. Topological properties

Topological properties measure the cooperation intensity between developers according to the network structure. Referring to the metric parameters of complex networks, this section gives the definitions of topological similarity between developers.

4.1.1. Developer strength

In order to facilitate calculation, this paper first normalizes the initial edge weights of the network, as shown in formula (1) (1)wij′=wijw∗where w∗=max{wij|eij∈E}. The purpose of normalization is to let all weights fall between 0 and 1, so that they have the same magnitude.

The developer strength is defined as the sum of all edge weights adjacent to developer vi, denoted as m(vi), i.e. (2)m(vi)=∑vj∈Γ(vi)wij′where Γ(vi) is the set of neighbor vertices of vi, which means for any vj∈Γ(vi), vi and vj have cooperative relationship.

Developer strength describes how tightly the developer is connected to surrounding network. In an unweighted network which only considers cooperative relationship, the edge weight is 1, and the developer strength is equal to the degree of developer, i.e., the number of neighbors in the network.

4.1.2. Direct connection strength

Direct connection strength considers the impact of direct connection between two vertices. When there has cooperative relationship between two developers (that is, there is an edge between corresponding vertices in the network), edge weight measures the connection strength between them. However, the weight can only represent the strength of two-point connection, and cannot reflect the influence of this strength. We expect to distinguish the different effects of all directly connected nodes for each node, which is the basis of similarity calculation. Therefore, this paper considers edge weight to define the direct connection strength, as shown in formula (3), where DI(vi,vj) is the direct connection strength of developers vi and vj. (3)DC(vi,vj)=wij′m(vi)+m(vj)−wij′ DC(vi,vj) measures the proportion of the edge weight between two developers in weights of their adjacent edges.

4.1.3. Indirect connection strength

Indirect connection strength considers the impact of indirect connection between two vertices. This paper draws on the acquaintance model in sociology: When A and B have a common friend C, A and B also have a chance to become friends. We find that neighbor nodes have an important impact on network connectivity. The more the co-partners (common neighbors) of two developers, the more intimate they are. Each collaborator contributes varying degree to the connection, which is related to the connection strength between developers and their neighbors. Let Γ(vi)∩Γ(vj) be the set of co-collaborators who collaborate with both developers vi and vj. To describe the indirect impact of their co-collaborator on two adjacent developers, we define (4)ϕij(vx)=wix′+wjx′m(vi)+m(vj)−wij′where vx∈Γ(vi)∩Γ(vj). ϕij(vx) reflect the connect strength of vi and vj via developer vx. Indirect connection strength between two developers is defined as the sum of connection strengths of all their co-collaborators, as shown in formula (5). (5)IC(vi,vj)=∑vx∈Γ(vi)∩Γ(vj)ϕij(vx)where IC(vi,vj) is the indirect connection strength of developers vi and vj. The more co-collaborators two developers have and the stronger the connection between co-developers is, the greater the indirect connection strength IC(vi,vj) is.

4.1.4. Topological similarity

For the software ecosystem, this paper classifies possible relationships between two developers as follows: ① neither direct nor indirect connect, ② indirect but direct connect, ③ direct but not indirect connect, and ④ both direct and indirect connect. The similarity between two developers increases in sequence with the number of classification. Based on the above discusses, this paper define the topological similarity between two developers according to their connection situation, as shown in formula (6). (6)SIMtpg(vi,vj)=0,eij∉E,Γ(vi)∩Γ(vj)=0̸IC(vi,vj),eij∉E,Γ(vi)∩Γ(vj)≠0̸DC(vi,vj),eij∈E,Γ(vi)∩Γ(vj)=0̸DC(vi,vj)+IC(vi,vj),eij∈E,Γ(vi)∩Γ(vj)≠0̸where SIMtop(vi,vj) is the topological similarity of developers vi and vj.

4.2. Semantic properties

Semantic properties measure the similarity of two developers participating in a collaborative repository. Referring to the interactive information in software ecosystem, this section gives definition and calculation of the semantic similarity between developers.

4.2.1. Shared repositories

Let U=(v1,v2,…,vm) be the set of developers we collect. For developer vi∈U, the repositories he participated in is called his participating repository set, denoted as PRi. The set of shared repositories of developers vi and vj is denoted as SRij, i.e. (7)SRij=PRi∩PRj

4.2.2. Familiarity probability of joint developers in shared repositories

The cooperation relationship considered in this paper is based on the cooperation of repository. If we want to mine the interaction between two developers in a repository, we can further mine the interaction degree only when they have a certain probability of interaction (i.e.the probability that they are familiar with in a repository). The probability of developers vi and vj being familiar in a repository involving one hundred people is much less than that in a repository involving only ten developers. It can be found that the familiarity of two developers in a repository is influenced by the total number of participants. Therefore, this paper introduces familiarity probability, as shown in formula (8) (8)Nrm(vi,vj)=2|crm|, Nrm(vi,vj) is the familiarity probability of joint developers vi and vj in shared repository rm, where rm∈Tij, and Crm is the set of developers participating in repository rm. Given a repository rm only shared by developers vi and vj, i.e. Crm={vi,vj}, N(vi,vj,rm)=2|Crm|=22=1. That is, vi and vj must be familiar with each other, which is also in line with common sense.

4.2.3. Jointly developers commit to shared repositories

The times of cooperation (edge weight in the network) is most commonly used for measuring the cooperation intensity between two developers. The times of cooperation is related to the number of repositories shared by two developers and the number of committing in each shared repository. The more repositories shared by two developer and the more frequent they commit to each shared repository, the greater the times of cooperation between them. Because cooperation is a bidirectional activity, when considering commit-based cooperative behavior in this paper, we need to consider the interaction between two developers in their commit records, i.e., the intersection part. Therefore, we use jointly commit to calculate the interaction degree between two developers in shared repositories, as shown in formula (9) (9)NCrm(vi,vj)=min{NCrm(vi),NCrm(vj)}where NCrm(vi) and NCrm(vj) are the committing numbers into rm by developer vi and vj, respectively. To facilitate the calculation, we also normalized the committing number, as shown in formula (10) (10)NCrm(vi,vj)′=NCrm(vi,vj)NC∗where NC∗=max{NCrm(vi,vj)|vi,vj∈V,rm∈Tij}.

4.2.4. Semantic similarity

Semantic metrics capture the amount of developers interaction. Usually the more interactions between two developers, the more familiar and similar they are. Based on the above definitions, the semantic similarity between developers vi and vj is defined as SIMsmt(vi,vj), as shown in formula (11) (11)SIMsmt(vi,vj)=∑rm∈TijNrm(vi,vj)×NCrm(vi,vj)′

4.3. Cooperation intensity

The cooperation intensity between developers is affected by both network structure and repository interaction. DC(vi,vj) is the cooperation intensity between two developers vi and vj which obtained by synthesizing the similarity of topological and semantic properties, as shown in formula (12) (12)DC(vi,vj)=λSIMtpg(vi,vj)+(1−λ)SIMsmt(vi,vj)where λ and 1−λ represent the weights of topological similarity and semantic similarity, respectively.

Community detection provides a clearer understanding of the relationships between developers for software ecosystem. Measuring the cooperation intensity by synthesizing network topology and interaction information can deeply explore the relationship between developers and make the result of community detection more accurate. On this basis, this paper proposes an algorithm, ABDCI, for community detection in software ecosystem.

5. Algorithm based on developer cooperation intensity

Hierarchical clustering algorithm has a good performance in community detection, among which Louvain algorithm [35] has received much attention because of its high efficiency and stability. It iterates by constantly merging communities with larger modularity gains. Louvain algorithm can be viewed as a two-level iterative merge process: the first stage considers each node as a community firstly, and merge nodes into the community with the largest module gain by calculating modularity function. The second stage is to compress the community formed by first stage into a new node, then repeating the iterative merge process of the first stage. Obviously, traditional methods only consider the topology of the network to detect the communities.

In this paper, we draw on the hierarchical clustering idea of Louvain algorithm and propose an novel community detection algorithm , ABDCI. The algorithm deeply explores the relationship between developers through topological and semantic properties, and comprehensively calculate the cooperation intensity between developers to update the network weight.

In the first stage of node merging, the cooperation intensity is used as the optimization function of node clustering to form the initial community, which makes the detection result more truly reflect the tightness between developers. Suppose that all vertices are divided into n communities in the first state, denoted as C1,C2,…,Cn.

In the second stage, some communities are merged intend to improve the total modularity. The module gain function by merging community Ci and Cj is shown in formula (13) (13)ΔQ=ki,j2M−2ki∗kj(2M)2where M is the total weight of all edges in the network, ki,j is the sum of edge weights from all nodes in Ci to all nodes in community Cj, ki is the sum of all edge weights in Ci, and kj is the sum of all edge weights in Cj.

The framework of the community detection algorithm ABDCI in this paper is shown as Fig. 1.

The specific implementation process of algorithm ABDCI is given as follows.

Input:

the software ecosystem network G=(V,E,W).

Output:

community division result {C1,C2,…,Cα}.

(1)

Assign each node in the network a community number, 1,2,…,m, where m is the total number of communities that equals the number of nodes in the initial division;

(2)

Construct the similarity matrix by calculating the cooperation intensity between developers according to formula (12);

(3)

Create an initial empty set of target nodes, denoted as T, and randomly select a node vi to join T, now vi is the current node,

(4)

Select the node vj which has the largest similarity with the current node according to the similarity matrix. if the node vj∉T, add vj to T, merge vj into the community that includes the current node, and vj becomes the current node now; if the node vj∈T, then randomly select a node in V−T to be the current node, where V−T represents the complementary set of T in set V, which is used to avoid repeated traversal.

(5)

Repeat (4) until T=V.

(6)

Consider each community formed by the above steps as a super point. Take the sum of edge weights in the same community as the weight of super point, and take the sum of edge weights of connected edges between communities as the weight between super points.

(7)

Calculate the value of modularity gain according to formula (13) after merging one super point (a community) neighbor super point (another community). If ΔQ is positive, the two communities are merged, otherwise they will not be merged.

(8)

Repeat (7) until the modularity gain for any two super points is negative.

(9)

Output the final result of community division.

6. Experimental analysis

6.1. Description of datasets

In order to verify the effectiveness of the method proposed in this paper, we choose GitHub, a popular code hosting platform, to collect data for experiments. GitHub is a more complex system with different types and sizes of software ecosystem, which is originally used for code sharing. Users can push code to it and collaborate with others on repositories. With the rapid increase in the number of developers and repositories, GitHub becomes the most popular and widely used system, from which we can construct various type of networks. To get the initial developer list and work repository information, this paper calls the GitHub API for data collection. Popular programming languages represent the mainstream development approach of this period. This paper synthesizes GitHub’s list of popular languages in recent years and chooses several popular languages. The more star signs a repository has, the more active and robust it will be and can provide us with more experimental data. Therefore, we choose repositories in each language by star ranking, and then get the list of contributors for each repository. When we collect a certain number of developers by repository, if there are repositories with amount participants, many developers will come from the same repository in our collect data, and their differentiation based on the cooperation intensity will be relatively small. In order to balance the distribution of people and ensure the diversity of data, for the large-scale repository with a large number of participants, two hundred developers with the greatest contribution are retained. We collect all commits for each developer from January 01, 2017 to September 04, 2019, and generate his commit record set. The developer collaboration network model constructed in this paper contains the following three kinds of information:

(1)

Repositories: Lists of repositories collected by star ranking in each language.

(2)

Developer Set: A collection of corresponding contributors collected by the above repository.

(3)

Working Set: A collection of all repositories committed by each developer in the developer set over a specific period of time.

6.2. Developer collaboration network

In order to verify the generalization of the proposed method, we construct various types and sizes of weighted developer collaboration networks based on social relations for different language systems. The definition of the network is as follows:

(1)

Each node in the developer collaboration network represents a single developer in GitHub.

(2)

Each edge in the developer collaboration network represents the joint commits of two developers to their shared repository.

(3)

Initial edge weight in the developer collaboration network represents cooperation times.

Based on the above information, we have five data acquisition. The first three times, for specific language systems: Javascript, Python, Ruby, respectively, obtain developers and their commit records in each language. In the last two times, we randomly choose developers who commit to repositories of different language types (that is, we remove the language type restriction on developers), so that the random network constructed will involve multiple languages. The second random data acquisition expands the scale of dataset on the basis of first time. We get the commit records of each developer, and then search for interaction through pairwise comparison. If they have joint commits, we add these two developers as nodes in the network and connect them. Those developers who do not have joint commits with others will be filtered out in the process of constructing network. By screening the initial dataset, the five developer collaboration networks finally constructed as shown in Table 1.

As can be seen from Table 1, compared with the random network, there is an obvious phenomenon of “rich community” in the independent language system network, and developers in the same language are prone to generate more interactions.

6.3. Evaluation indicator

Newman and Girvan first proposed the concept of modularity that is a commonly used indicator to measure the tightness of community structure in network [26]. For weighted network, Ji proposed the concept of weighted modularity as an evaluation indicator [36]. Since the software ecosystem studied in this paper is considered as a weighted network, we use weighted modularity to analyze the community division results, which is defined as formula (14) (14)Q=12W∑1≤i,j≤m(ωij−kikj2W)δ(Ci,Cj)where ωij is the edge weight between node i and node j, W is the sum of the weights of all edges, ki represents the total weights of all edges that connected to node i, Ci and Cj are the communities to which node vi and node vj belong, respectively, and δ(Ci,Cj)=1,Ci=Cj0,Ci≠Cj

In actual network, when the value of modularity is between 0.3 and 0.7, it is considered that the community structure is much good. The larger the value of modularity, the clearer the community structure is.

6.4. Experimental results and analysis

To verify the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm, two groups of experiments are designed in this paper. Firstly, we analyze the influence of weight λ in formula (12) on the results of community division in developer collaboration network, i.e. the clarity of community structure under different weights between topological similarity and semantic similarity. In the second group of experiment, three classic community detection algorithms are selected to compare with the algorithm proposed in this paper.

6.4.1. The influence of variable weights

In order to find a suitable weighting factor, this paper takes 7 different values from 0 to 1 for weight λ in formula (12) to analyze the change of community division. The experimental results of five developer collaboration networks are shown in Fig. 2, Fig. 3, respectively. The transverse axis is the values of λ, and the vertical axis represents the number of communities and the value of modularity, respectively.

As can be seen from Fig. 2, For G1−G4 with similar scale, the number of communities shows the same trend, that is, with the increase of λ, the number of divided communities gradually decreases. For the network with one to two thousands nodes, the number of communities divided by the algorithm ABDCI is mostly in the range of (20,50). For G5, when λ increases from 0 to 0.8, the number of divided communities decreases, while the number of communities when λ=1 is greater than that when λ=0.8. The maximum number of communities which the algorithm ABDCI divides is 131, and the minimum number is 69. To sum up, we can see that network size and λ value will have a certain impact on the number of divided communities.

As can be seen from Fig. 3, for each network, the modularities are at a low level when λ gets two extremum values (λ=0,λ=1). λ=0 represents only considering the influence of semantic properties, while λ=1 represents only considering the influence of topological properties on the cooperation intensity of developers. In addition, it can be seen that when λ=0.7, the modularities of G1−G3 reach the maximum values, which are 0.6892, 0.5972 and 0.4963, respectively. In other word, when topology and semantic properties account for 70% and 30% respectively, the effect of detection is the best. While for G4 and G5, their modularities get maximum when λ=0.5, which are 0.7503 and 0.4899, respectively. That is when the weight ratio of the topology properties and the semantic properties are each 0.5, the clearest community structure can be identified. By comparing the maximum modularity of the five networks in Fig. 3, it can be found that G2 has the largest modularity, its edge density is obviously lower than other networks. By comparing the five broken lines in Fig. 3 we can find that the networks of three independent language systems have a similar trend, while the two random networks have another similar trend. From the experimental results, we can conclude that no matter what type of network, the more clear community structure can be detected when combine topology and semantic properties than only consider single properties. In addition, network type and edge intensity are two important factors affecting detect results.

The community division results of G1−G3 when λ=0.7 and G4−G5 when λ=0.5 are displayed using tool Gephi, shown as Fig. 4, Fig. 5, Fig. 6, Fig. 7, Fig. 8 in which communities are distinguished with different colors.

To verify the validity of the proposed algorithm ABDCI (for G1−G3 λ takes 0.7 and for G4−G5 λ takes 0.5), three classical community detection algorithms are selected for comparison: Louvain algorithm [35], LPA [37], and weighted GN algorithm [28]. The weighted modularity defined in formula (14) is used to evaluate the division results of different methods. The final results of these four algorithms are shown as Fig. 9.

As can be seen from Fig. 9, the performance of lougain, LPA and weighted GN algorithms is unstable on five networks. Among them, LPA performs best on G1, and gets the highest module value of community detection, which is 0.6043; for G4, weighted GN algorithm performs well in the three algorithms, and its module value is 0.7239; Louvain algorithm performs better on G2, G3 and G5 than the other two algorithms, and its module values for the three network communities are 0.6771, 0.4198 and 0.3921, respectively. Except for the module value of G1 is slightly lower than LPA, the algorithm ABDCI proposed in this paper is better than other algorithms on the other four networks, especially in G3 and G5, it shows obvious advantages. The modularity values of algorithm ABDCI on five networks are 0.5972, 0.6892, 0.4963, 0.7503 and 0.4899, respectively. The above results prove that comprehensively considering topological properties and semantic properties of the developer collaboration network in software ecosystem can identify a clearer community structure than unilateral consideration of network topology.

7. Threats to the validity

This section proposes main threats to the validity of the experiments and the methods of addressing them.

First of all, there are internal threats. The interaction between developers is complex, so it is difficult to find a reasonable quantitative way to deeply explore their potential relationship. In order to avoid the one-sidedness of traditional topology model, this paper adds semantic properties analysis. We define the topological similarity function and semantic similarity function from two aspects of network link and repository participation respectively for quantitative analysis. The experimental results of variable weights show that the proposed method performs better than the single topology measurement.

Secondly, threats from outside. In this paper, we propose a new community detection method for developer cooperation network in software ecosystem, and we can only show the effectiveness of the method if we apply it to various types of networks. In this paper, three popular language networks and two random networks are constructed. By comparing algorithm experiments on five networks, it is proved that the proposed method can achieve better performance than some classical algorithms on different types of developer collaboration networks.

Additionally, this method needs to be further verified in terms of network type and scale. On the one hand, only three popular language systems are selected in this paper, and many other language systems (Java, C, PHP, etc.) are not used in experiments. On the other hand, although the effectiveness of the proposed method in two different scales random networks is preliminarily proved, the above conclusions cannot be directly extended to larger networks. Therefore, the method proposed in this paper needs to be further tested in practice.

8. Conclusion

This paper aims at solving the community detection problem of real developer collaboration network in software ecosystem. Considering the shortcomings of single properties of nodes in traditional complex networks, this paper defines the cooperation intensity of developers by synthesizing the semantic properties of developers and the topological properties of the network, and proposes a community detection algorithm ABDCI based on the cooperation intensity of developers. Experiments on different types of networks in software ecosystem show that the proposed algorithm can effectively identify a clear community structure. There are various social relationships among developers in software ecosystem. Based on different social relationships, community structures with different characteristics can be identified. The knowledge of complex networks can be used to mine community organizations under different relationships. This is the next step for us.
