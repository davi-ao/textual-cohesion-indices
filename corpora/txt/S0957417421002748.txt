Intensification of research and development activities as a consequence of knowledge explosion demands professionalization in most of the aspects related to such activities. Knowledge explosion also ensued the rise of many research fields including several interdisciplinary ones. An associated increase in joint-venture research projects and hence a hike in the volume of research collaborations in both academia as well as industries is also witnessed. Recommending or predicting potential collaborators might be useful for researchers for forming new groups (Lopes, Moro, Da Silva, Barbosa, & de Oliveira, 2011) and be decisive for every stage of research that spans from the planning of research and developing research proposal of substantial quality to activities that ensures visibility and reach of their published or patented works. According to Abramo, D’Angelo, and Di Costa (2009), in academia, collaboration is found to be beneficial to the productivity of researchers. As inventions and innovations are the backbone of any innovation ecosystem and industry, collaborative R&D activities that may lead to patents is the key to sustain any industry or innovation ecosystem. An investigation by Favaro, Ninka, and Turvani (2012) concluded that inventor collaborations have sizable productivity effects on innovation. As the identification of the most suitable potential collaborations is the key to successful collaborative ventures, recommendation systems that can do so can be vital for sustaining innovation ecosystems and industries.
Several recommendation systems are available in arenas like e-commerce for product/service recommendation, social networks (Liu & Lee, 2010) for the recommendation of individuals/organizations. Recommendation system development can be based on analytics and sometimes recommendation systems serve as tools for analytics too, like the one by Kaklauskas et al. (2013) to analyze student’s academic performance. Potential of network-based solutions like link prediction for the design of collaboration recommendation systems is discussed by Lü and Zhou (2011) among other applications of link prediction in complex networks. In the case of social networks, link prediction based recommendation in homogeneous and heterogeneous networks is a recognized problem (Dong et al., 2012, Kong et al., 2019). The gravity of collaboration recommendation system development eventuated some research efforts on collaboration recommendation systems. The challenges in the recommendation of new collaborations in academia are discussed in comparison to the friendship recommendations in Social Networks by Lopes, Moro, Wives, and De Oliveira (2010). Recent work on university-industry collaboration recommendation on R&D projects using a context-aware system (Wang, Ma, Liao, & Du, 2017) requires both online and offline modules to perform the task effectively. Some of the important works (discussed in Section 2) related to recommendation based on link prediction were found to be oriented around an approach known as supervised topological approach. The importance of incorporation of semantic parameters, especially the semantic proximity in terms of the research area, geographical location, etc., for ensuring the effectiveness of recommendations forms the crux of a major approach for recommendation system (Brandão, Moro, Lopes, & Oliveira, 2013), namely link semantics (Halkidi, Nguyen, Varlamis, & Vazirgiannis, 2003). The effective recommendation system design presented by Brandão et al. (2013) was conceived for academic collaborations. The development of a recommendation system for inventors remains underexplored except for a link semantic recommendation system by Wu, Sun, and Tang (2013) and our discussion on related literature might bring clarity about this gap. Along with the above-mentioned benefits of collaboration recommendation systems, such systems might also be helpful for inventors who are in various phases of their careers to advance their careers. For instance, a novice inventor might be able to know which inventor (at a higher stage of career) from the same or related fields to collaborate with and plan the apt set of skills to be acquired to do so. This is one of the motivations behind our present research. Though hybrid recommendation system design by Brandão et al. (2013) is free of supervised learning and is supposed to be of high usability, the dependency on so many contextual semantic variables such as the proximity of authors through affiliations of common co-authors, co-operation, social closeness, and correlational aspects (Lopes et al., 2010) and the associated requirement for collection and management of data from multiple sources challenges its usability as a general framework for recommendation system in any research context. The only existing recommendation system framework for inventor collaboration, i.e, the framework by Wu et al. (2013) is intense on link semantics and also require supervised learning. Therefore, the major objective of this research is to develop a framework that might serve as the base for a fully functional inventor collaboration recommendation system with the following capabilities:
(i) Ability to toss diverse and novel recommendations that may benefit a large number of inventors (i.e., system should have high novelty, diversity, and coverage).
(ii) Ability to work well irrespective of the field/context of analysis with high ‘ease of use’ (i.e., the system should have high context-independent usability).
Such a framework should be based on a diligent design. The rationale behind the design of our framework can be stated as “Stronger the co-inventorship relationship between the common co-inventors of a pair of inventors and stronger the coupling between a pair of inventors through technological fields, stronger the potential for them to collaborate to generate successful technological innovations”. As ‘network theory’ is a reasonably well-developed field of research and as it forms the basis of our framework, the practical methods that we use have strong theoretical roots and backing. For instance, the theoretical justification of major link prediction methods including the usage of common neighbors in networks was given by Sarkar, Chakrabarti, and Moore (2011). Though ‘network theory’ is a field of great potential, it is relatively less explored for the development of inventor collaboration recommendation systems that can be beneficial to the innovation ecosystems and society at large. This gap between theory and practice is successfully bridged in this work. Thus, the novelty of this research lies in the development of a framework that has high usability due to the minimized dependency on semantics and the absence of supervised learning techniques. As the framework uses Minimal Link Semantic (MLS) approach, it can be termed as MLS framework. The MLS framework, which is built on a sheer network approach that ensures simplicity in implementation and operation, is expected to be capable of overcoming the challenge of research context-independent usability.
For extracting indications for identification of links for recommendations, the affiliation networks of patents and inventors (PI) and patents and technological field (PF) are used. Another important concern that surfaces is how to determine the direction of recommendation (i.e., which inventor to be recommended to whom). This can be identified by using the metric harmonic receptivity index introduced by George, Lathabai, Prabhakaran, and Changat (2020), which reflects the receptivity of the inventions co-invented by an inventor for a particular field (indicated by citations from other patents in that research context) when harmonic credit allocation scheme (Hodge et al., 1981, Hagen, 2008) is used. Thus, the improved framework for contextual productivity assessment developed by George et al. (2020), which is bias-free to a great extent than the framework by Lathabai, Prabhakaran, and Changat (2017), is embedded in the proposed recommendation system. An important feature of the proposed recommendation system is that, by its diligent design, high priority recommendations and low priority recommendations can be made. Another notable fact is that, unlike previous approaches that mostly used supervised learning methods, our approach does not use supervised learning methods. Therefore, evaluation metrics such as accuracy-related metrics used in those works cannot be used for the evaluation of our approach. The accuracy of the system cannot be verified at this stage and only post-implementation verification is possible. Other important characteristics of recommendation systems like novelty, coverage, and diversity were evaluated using suitable metrics after demonstrating the framework using the case study. Novelty indicates the ability of the system to toss recommendations that are otherwise unknown (i.e., not visible) to the inventors. Coverage implies the ability of the system to toss distinct recommendations so that the benefit of recommendations is enjoyed by more users. Diversity is evaluated in terms of the system’s ability to toss recommendations to and from inventors belonging to different phases of their careers. To the best of our knowledge, no other frameworks were evaluated or demonstrated the ability to provide recommendations to and about inventors belonging to different career phases. Details of the evaluation metrics are given in Section 5. High priority recommendations are found to be of high novelty and diversity and low priority recommendations are found to be of relatively less novelty and diversity as expected. Due to the simplicity and flexibility of the design and the ability to toss novel and diverse recommendations (to inventors who are in various phases of their career), the proposed framework seems to be of much higher usability than the existing frameworks.

Network concepts used for the design of the recommendation system framework are discussed in Section 3. A detailed discussion of the methodology for the design of the recommendation system can be found in Section 4. Research collaborations in academia and industry are very vital for almost all the stakeholders and are therefore heavily studied from various perspectives. Under the lens of innovation and technology management, collaborations are mainly studied about the knowledge transfer as well as technology transfer. Studies on collaboration in scientometric perspective primordially deal with the exploration of the role of scientific and technological collaborations for understanding the internal dynamics of science and technology and for the formulation of S&T policies (Melin & Persson, 1996). As our work is about the development of a framework for a collaboration recommendation system for inventors, it is more oriented towards the scientometric perspective and a detailed discussion of developments in the scientometric perspective and works that deal with related concerns is carried out.
Collaboration relationship in science is widely explored for various purposes, especially in the form of author collaboration networks or co-authorship networks. According to Beaver and Rosen (1978), co-authorships in science originated and is still in effect as a response to the professionalization of science. Price’s pioneering work- Little Science, Big Science (de Solla Price, 1963), distinguished both the terms by attributing the term ‘Big science’ to larger teamwork and the term ‘Little science’ to much small collaborating group’s work. Kuhn also recognized the importance of the relationship among authors and the formation of scientific communities working within a paradigm Kuhn (2012). One of the earlier attempts to measure collaboration through co-authorship was by Melin and Persson (1996), which discussed the need to survey and follow up issues of collaboration, the relationship between collaboration and co-authorship, the nature of bibliometric data, and how various aspects of collaboration can be analyzed through the refined use of co-authorship bibliometrics. Katz and Martin (1997) found that co-authorship is no more than a partial indicator of collaboration and therefore co-authorship based collaboration studies are prone to the research culture and practices among the individuals or organizations in different disciplines. Despite these limitations, they admitted that co-authorship networks are much effective in the investigation of patterns of scientific collaboration and its dynamics, international collaboration in science and its growth, behavioral patterns of collaborating scientists, individual productivity studies, etc. Investigation of research collaboration on the individual perspective by Melin (2000) revealed strong pragmatism and a high degree of self-organization as the major characterization of collaborations. The extent to which research collaboration can enhance or generate S&T (Scientific and Technical) human capital (Bozeman, Dietz, & Gaughan, 2001) was studied by Bozeman and Corley (2004) and a high variance was found to exist among collaborations. Patterns in scientific collaboration and how such patterns vary among disciplines over time is studied by Newman (2004). The need for systematic quantitative monitoring of inputs and outcomes of collaboration through bibliometric surveys is emphasized by Glänzel and Schubert (2004). Growth of international collaboration in science was explored using network structure in all fields of science in 1990 and 2000 and suggested the existence of self-organization and dynamics at the sub-field level operating in all fields of science as the cause for likely emergence of the growth of international collaboration (Wagner & Leydesdorff, 2005). Assessment of level of correlation, at the institution level, between scientific productivity and collaboration intensity (both international and private organizational) in the Italian academic research system, could not find any evidence of the existence of a correlation between extramural research collaboration and overall performance of research institution (Abramo et al., 2009). Using a hybrid network by combining co-authorship and citation network related to various Physical Review journals, the extent of certain tendencies among authors to cite themselves, their co-authors and reciprocate the citations received from another scientist were studied by Martin, Ball, Karrer, and Newman (2013). A recent study on the causal relationship of co-authorship and individual productivity among economists indicated that, after controlling the effect of endogenous co-authorship formation, unobservable heterogeneity, and time-varying factors, a positive effect of intellectual collaboration on individual performance can be observed (Ductor, 2015). An attempt to map the patterns and evolution of collaborative behavior across scientific disciplines and its association with productivity can be found in the work of Parish, Boyack, and Ioannidis (2018). These are some of the major works on scientific collaboration in scientometric or related perspective. When it comes to the study of collaboration in technology, relatively fewer scientometric studies are found about inventor collaboration using patent literature than author collaboration using scientific literature. Such attempts are discussed next.
Co-inventorship networks based studies for identification of productive inventors (Chang, Wu, & Leu, 2010), for mapping collaborative knowledge production in a country/region (Gao, Guan, & Rousseau, 2011), for exploring the influence of inventor productivity on network structure and behavior (Harhoff, Heibel, & Hoisl, 2013), etc., were some of the major works. Importance of geography (Christ, 2009) or regional proximity for collaboration and effect/role of collaboration in regional inventive performance (de Araújo, Gonçalves, & Taveira, 2019) were also among the explored topics. Recent work on the community structure of the co-inventorship network by Doonan, Higham, Governale, and Zülicke (2019) suggested that the first citation time of patents is significantly related to the inventor community structure. Co-inventorship based study of inter-firm inventor mobility and its role in generating high-impact innovation was investigated by Tóth and Lengyel (2019). The need and importance of the inventor collaboration recommendation system are once again indicated (though implicitly) by this study. If inter-firm mobility can be analyzed and its relation/effect on the production of high impact innovation can be studied and if such investigations are of immense benefit, couldn’t the notion of inventor recommendation which is based on co-inventorship prediction be much insightful for inventors to plan their career and improve their potential for mobility? Despite the worth of such investigations, when it comes to the design of a collaboration recommendation system for inventors, we could find only one work-i.e., work by Wu et al. (2013), which directly addressed the recommendation problem using the link prediction approach. The model framework developed comprised of three main steps- candidate generation, candidate refinement, and interactive learning. As several similarity attributes which include the semantic ones that cannot be identified from the structure of the network are used for candidate generation and sophisticated learning methods are used for ranking candidates for refinement and online updating based on inventor feedback, that approach can be regarded as a hybrid approach or link semantic approach. Like the efficient academic collaboration recommendation framework by Brandão et al. (2013), the usability of the framework by Wu et al. (2013) is also susceptible to ‘usability challenge’ due to intense dependence on semantic parameters. Therefore, a framework with high usability and less dependence on semantic parameters is required.
As the link prediction approach can offer promising designs for collaboration recommendation systems, such attempts in literature are analyzed next. Pujari and Kanawati, 2015, Meghanathan, 2016 reported that link prediction approaches can be classified into two, namely, node-features based approaches and topological approaches. Hybrid approaches of the two are also found in the literature. In node-features based approaches, along with the information about the structure of the network, additional information about nodes of the network (often semantic or content-based variables) is utilized for link prediction as done by Wang, Satuluri, and Parthasarathy (2007). These are usually done when the graph or underlying structure of the networks are too sparse. Topological approaches exploit the network structure only (i.e., establishment and variation of connections over time) for retrieval of relevant information for link prediction. These are again classified into- (i) temporal and static, based on whether the dynamic aspects of network is considered, (ii) dyadic and community/subgraph based and global, based on the way of computation of various scores, (iii) supervised, unsupervised or semisupervised based on the kind of learning method used for the prediction model. Temporal methods take into account the dynamics while non-temporal/static approaches do not consider the dynamics. In the dyadic approach, scores are locally computed for pairs (i.e., for both nodes in a link), whereas the community/subgraph approach computes the scores at the community/subgraph level. For dyadic topological approach, indices based on neighborhood such as common neighbors (common co-authors in case of co-authorship network) used by Newman (2004), fractionally counted common neighbors as in Adamic Adar co-efficient (Adamic & Adar, 2003) & Resource Allocation (RA) index (Ou, Jin, Zhou, Wang, & Yin, 2007), etc., were much explored. Global approach based score computation examines the entire network structure. In an unsupervised approach, a few attribute scores which are usually ranked, are used for the prediction of unlinked node pairs and the key highlight is that learning of a model is not required. Many topological attribute scores along with ground truth about network structure (labeled data) are used for model creation in a supervised approach. Semisupervised approaches make use of partially labeled training data for model creation. Following this, a notable work by (Pavlov & Ichise, 2007) can be placed in the category of supervised topological approach. Liben-Nowell and Kleinberg (2007) conducted a systematic comparison of similarity indices (derived from network structure) that can be used for link prediction and identified the suitable indices to be chosen for training to produce more accurate prediction results. Pujari and Kanawati (2013) presented a co-authorship link prediction by considering many relationships in scientific literature and modeling these as multiplex networks, a kind of multi-layer networks in which different relationships among the same nodes are represented in different layers. The approach seems to be capable of computing topological attributes of each layer and also directly computing the multiplex based attributes of potential links. Above works that are based on the supervised topological approach and are thereby limited by the drawbacks of supervised learning and the usability (one of the important requirements of information systems) is susceptible. Despite the tests about efficiency, information about the implementation of such frameworks in academic recommendation system products or usage in online academic social networks is not available. The link semantic framework developed by Brandão et al. (2013) seems to be a hybrid approach as it uses semantic features along with exploiting structural attributes of the co-authorship network. However, as discussed in Section 1, the usage of so many semantic parameters might affect its usability as a general framework for the context-independent recommendation. So a recommendation system of less complexity and much usability is to be conceived to ensure context-independent applications. Exploration of possibilities of topological approaches that could provide a substantial indication of likely collaboration without much analytical effort is felt.
Among the above-discussed approaches for link prediction, our approach can be regarded as a dyadic unsupervised topological approach. The key difference is that we do not merely rely on the common co-actor (co-author/co-inventor) count, instead, we take into account how strongly a pair of co-actors collaborate with these common co-actors. Apart from that, how strongly a pair of co-actors are coupled to different common research fields is also taken to consideration. Here, only the affiliation between the work (either paper/patent) to that of field and inventors are captured and no semantic technologies are used for determination of the terms that designate fields because the terms are source-given (found at the same database where other details are found). Although our framework is purely a network-based one, we still chose the term ‘Minimal Link Semantic approach’, because of the usage of terms that indicate research fields, which some might argue as semantic terms. However, as our design is done in such a way to incorporate flexibility for future improvements, determination of sub-field designation through semantics can be attempted for the refinement of the system.
As our recommendation system framework is a network-based one, important networks and concepts that are required for building the framework are discussed first. Some variables that reflect the direct as well as the indirect relationship in the body of technological knowledge represented by patent literature, are vital for the determination of the output variable, namely, potential collaborations to be recommended. Major direct input variables are (i) Affiliation of patents to inventors, (ii) Affiliation of patents to research fields, and (iii) Citation relationship among patents. Co-inventorship relations (existing collaborations), plays a major role along with field coupling relationship. Both of them are indirect or derived variables that function as auxiliary or intermediate variables. All these variables can be structurally represented as networks. The general definition of networks and the definition of the networks associated with the key variables are discussed as follows.

A patent citation network is a 1-mode network with patents are vertices and citations among patents as directed links or arcs. As our interest lies in inventor collaboration, the collaboration network discussed here will be inventor collaboration networks. In response to the limitations of major productivity indices for actors like authors, journals, etc., for contextual productivity assessment, a network-based framework that can generate contextual productivity indices was developed by Lathabai et al. (2017). Receptivity index or Re which is the total number of citations in a research theme or context is one of the indices that can be generated. It is done by creating 2-mode networks of papers and authors/journals (which is unweighted) and by converting these networks to weighted 2-mode networks. To convert this network to a weighted network, we have to either explore the properties of this network (implicitly) or explore the properties of the constituent entities of the network such as vertices or links externally. As one set of the vertices (papers) are related by citation relationship and forms a citation network, the values of attributes of works identified by the citation network can be used to convert the 2-mode affiliation network to a weighted network. The process of conversion is termed as injection because, the values of attributes of vertices in the first mode (i.e., indegree values that reflects the citations of papers) are introduced or injected into the unweighted 2-mode network as the weight of arcs. For instance, let there be a paper b and author c in an unweighted paper-author 2-mode network. As the network is the unweighted weight of arc from b to c will be 1. Now, in the citation network, if b has got n citations (computed as indegree or the number of incoming arcs of citations to b), the weight of arc from b to c will have the weight n. This framework considers the full counting of authorship credit. i.e., for a paper co-authored by two or more authors, each author will be assigned equal and full credit of authorship when there may be a disparity in their level of contribution. This might result in favor or bias for some of the authors because their productivity may be inflated than the actual. Thus, the need for a bias-free productivity assessment of the framework was felt, and this gap was attempted to bridged by George et al. (2020). The bias-free framework was designed for the capability to consider counting schemes like fractional counting and harmonic counting that could reduce the effect of biases and select the scheme that can achieve maximum reduction of such effect. Fractional counting assigns fractional but equal credits to all the actors (all the authors of a co-authored paper or all the inventors of a co-invented patent). With fractional counting, though sharing of credit equally among actors reduces the inflationary bias significantly, a kind of bias named equalizing bias still exists. In most cases, the order of the appearance of the actor name in the byline information of paper or patent document may reflect the level of contribution of actors. Therefore, the actual credit of authorship/inventorship of paper/patent that can be attributed to actors might decline from first author/inventor to the last author/inventor in a paper/patent. With fractional counting, as every actor is given equal credit, both inflationary and deflationary effects can be observed. For actors whose names occur first or close to first in the sequence, equal sharing of credit may result in deflation. For those actors whose names are closer to the last name in the sequence, inflation can be observed. This kind of equalizing bias (a term attributed to the equal sharing credit in fractional counting) will be less for a counting scheme like harmonic counting that distributes the credit unequally and in a decreasing fashion as we go from first to last in the sequence of names. Now, we can examine how the proposed framework tosses recommendations.
As discussed earlier, the MLS framework utilizes (i) strength of derived co-inventorship relation between a pair of inventors through their common co-inventors and (ii) strength of coupling of a pair of inventors through common technological fields, for predicting links that represent potential collaborations. The strength of the derived co-inventorship relation will be reflected in the edges (undirected links) of the Inventor-Inventor derived network. Steps 2 and 3 of the algorithm creates an inventor collaboration network or co-inventorship network (please see Section 3.3 for definition and details). It will be an undirected network and the weight of each edge represent the number of together patents co-invented by the pair of vertices that form that edge. The collaboration distance of inventors connected by an edge will be 1. The collaboration distance of a pair of inventors sharing a common co-inventor will be 2. Steps 4 and 5 of the algorithm creates derived Inventor-Inventor network as discussed in Section 3.4. It will be an undirected network showing the relationship between inventors separated by distance = 2 in the collaboration network. Now the case study of ‘Wireless power transmission’ is discussed for demonstrating the MLS framework for inventor collaboration recommendation.
‘Wireless Power Transmission’ (WPT) is a set of technologies that signifies wireless power transference (transmission and distribution) at any level. Some of the early experiments in WPT were done by Moore, 1904, Tesla, 1908, Tesla, 1914. Nikola Tesla, the founder of AC electricity, deserves a great deal of credit in conceiving WPT too. WPT is supposed to be reliable, efficient, fast, and have low maintenance costs once it is developed to its fullest potential. WPT is useful for both short-range or long-range transmissions. Conventionally, electricity is manufactured from different sources and is delivered to various destinations through wires and cables. Wired transmission (WT) is prone to many disadvantages such as (i) heavy transmission loss, (ii) immense cost of the system infrastructure that includes high-tension transmission line cables, substations, and towers, and diminished economic feasibility in electrifying remote areas and (iii) huge environmental impact due to the disposal of used wires. Now the evaluation of the recommendation system framework is carried out.
Recommendation system performance is mostly evaluated using attributes such as accuracy, novelty, coverage and diversity.
By the term accuracy, the accuracy in the performance of the recommendation system, i.e., the accuracy of recommendations made is implied. Precision and Recall are two important metrics related to accuracy. For recommendation system evaluation, the original definition of precision and recall for information retrieval by Kent, Berry, Luehrs, and Perry (1955) are used after modifying ‘retrieved documents’ and ‘relevant documents’ as ‘retrieved recommendations’ and ‘relevant recommendations’ respectively. Precision indicates accuracy as the fraction of retrieved relevant recommendations (true positive) in the total recommendations made (true positive + false positive). Recall on other hand evaluates the accuracy in terms of the fraction of retrieved relevant recommendations (true positive) in the total possible relevant recommendations (i.e., the total recommendations that turned out to be relevant (true positive) along with the ones that not recommended by the system but turned out to be relevant (false negative)). From these, it is clear that a proper criterion should be there to determine the relevancy of the recommendations based on the purpose intended to be served by the system. Approaches that use supervised learning usually treat a portion of ground data (labeled data of collaborations) as relevant against which the measures of accuracy are computed. As our approach does not use learning methods, the accuracy of the framework cannot be computed using the above metrics, at this stage. Even if we wait for some time to check whether some of the retrieved recommendations about collaboration have materialized, then also such data of actual collaborations cannot be treated as total possible relevant recommendations and compute true positives and false negatives. Such collaborations can only be treated as total possible relevant recommendations if the recommendations retrieved by the framework are communicated to the identified target inventors before the period of the waiting. That means implementation should be complete well before the evaluation. As a post-implementation evaluation of accuracy is only possible for our framework, such an attempt is reserved as a further endeavor. Evaluation for novelty, coverage, and diversity of the framework is possible and such an evaluation is discussed next.
Opportunities for collaborations, i.e., information on inventors with which collaboration can be attempted, that is not visible to the actors without the help of recommendation algorithms can be termed as novel recommendations (Fouss and Saerens, 2008, Brandão et al., 2013) and the evaluation of the ability of a system to toss such recommendations can be termed as Novelty evaluation. The novelty of the recommendation system can be evaluated in terms of the ability of the system to recommend ‘not-so-often’ recommended actors. This is based on the assumption that ‘often’ recommended actors might be known or visible to other actors. For the evaluation of novelty of the recommendations made in the case study, we use the novelty index described by Brandão et al. (2013). Novelty index uses the frequency of inventors f in sets of recommended inventors and also communicated inventors to judge whether the inventor is known or not known. As discussed in the introduction, coverage indicates the ability of the system to toss more distinct recommendations (Gunawardana & Shani, 2015) so that the benefits of the recommendation may not be confined to some of the inventors. Most of the studies evaluate coverage in terms of the recommended actors only assuming that users need not or might not view other users who are communicated. On the other hand, if policymakers are interested to know/foresee or assess/weigh the possibilities of potential collaborations, information about both the recommended inventors and the communicated inventors might be needed. In such cases, the coverage of both these sets has to be checked. So we examine the coverage of both recommended set as well as communicated set, unlike our predecessors. Major coverage index like Gini index give an idea about the coverage within a set and hence we use that as an indicator or intra-set coverage. However, one of the major features of our recommendation framework is that both high priority and low priority recommendations are tossed, and hence four different sets of inventors- high priority recommended inventors, high priority communicated inventors, low priority recommended inventors, and low priority communicated inventors, are formed. We need to assess whether there is enough distinction between these sets and otherwise there is a high chance for these sets to be occupied by a few inventors so that benefits won’t reach beyond them. This requires the assessment of inter-set coverage. Jaccard dissimilarity index can be used for this purpose. The details of computation of both intra-set and inter-set coverage indices are given next.

Diversity of the recommendation system is the measure of the ability of the system to toss diverse recommendations, i.e., recommendations to and from inventors that belong to different classes/groups. Definition of classes or groups should be done rationally and pragmatically. Previous works, especially the academic collaboration recommendation system research used field/subfield of activity as a criterion for the classification of inventors. However, as we used the technological field coupling of inventors as one of the means for the retrieval of recommendations, the assessment of diversity using fields is not justifiable. Another important argument is that unlike academia, working on different related or compatible fields simultaneously by establishing co-authorship links is not easy for inventive researchers and therefore, field/subfield diversity assessment for inventors has its limitations. One useful and informative way to assess the diversity of the framework is by investigating the ability of the framework to toss recommendations to and from inventors belonging to various phases of their career. Classification of inventors based on their experience (active years of patenting activity) seems to be rational. However, the number of career phases and time-window allocated for each phase of career should be done per the nature of the technology area under consideration. In the case of WPT, the area is an emerging one and serious and continuous patent activities commenced roughly within the last 25–30 years. So the following four phases of a career can be thought of:
Early career phase- if experience lies from 0 to 10 years, denoted by alphabet E.
Moderate career phase- if experience lies from 11 to 15 years, denoted by alphabet M.
Moderately advanced career phase- if experience lies from 16 to 20 years, denoted by MA.
Advanced career phase- if experience is greater than 20 years, denoted by A.

From the above computations, the high priority recommendations and communications are found to be highly diverse and low priority recommendations and communications are relatively less diverse. Like the case of novelty, diversity scores for low priority recommendations and communications might get improved on lowering the threshold. However, such an exploration is beyond the scope of this work. From the present scores itself, the ability of the recommendation system to toss recommendations to and from inventors in various career phases is undoubtedly established. Thus, from the evaluation of the framework using aspects like novelty, coverage, and diversity, the overall ability of the framework is found to be satisfactory, and therefore, the objectives of this research are successfully achieved. These abilities along with the simplicity (due to the less/no dependency on semantic parameters and freedom from the need for processing information from multiple databases) and flexibility (for incorporating suitable semantic parameters in case if required) in design immensely contribute to better usability of the system.
Due to the ever-increasing volume of R&D in both academia and industry, Research management (RM) systems are steadily becoming and are evolving in response to the changing needs of various stakeholders. As some of these needs arise from the prevalence of collaborative research, collaboration management has become an important concern of RM. Given the importance of determining potential collaborations, collaboration prediction or recommendation has to be an integral part of RM systems. Though the link semantic approach is found to be effective for the design of an author collaboration recommendation system, the existing design that depends on many attributes of authors like co-authorship, geographical locations, etc. for computing link semantics-based metrics incurs limited usability. Another important limitation behind the implementation that affects usability is the need for data from multiple sources. The existing framework that directly addressed the inventor recommendation problem is also prone to ‘usability challenge’ due to slightly high dependence on semantics and sophisticated learning methods. Inspired by this, a framework for an inventor collaboration recommendation system with high usability and capability to recommend collaborations for inventors at all stages of career is conceived for the first time according to the best of our knowledge. For ensuring the usability of the inventor collaboration system to a standard level and to keep the analytic effort required to a comfortable level, the number of link semantic parameters should be limited and the need for data collection from multiple sources should be eliminated if possible. As limiting the usage of link semantic parameters may raise the concern of ending up with less feasible or misleading results, the design of a recommendation system with fewer semantic parameters should be diligent enough to address these concerns to a great extent. The rationale behind the MLS framework which is based on two factors- the strength of potential co-inventorship through shared common co-inventors and strength of potential co-inventorship through technological field coupling of inventors, ensures feasibility. Also, there is almost no chance of missing out on high potential collaborations. Moreover, as special care is ensured to avoid redundant recommendations (recommendation of already existing collaboration), the recommendation system design is ought to be efficient. Usage of a state-of-the-art methodology for bias-free contextual productivity assessment of inventors to determine the direction of recommendations is an added advantage.
Upon the evaluation of a real-world case study of a technological area namely- ‘Wireless Power Transmission’, using popular indicators that measure the novelty, coverage, and diversity, the effectiveness of the system in terms of these aspects is confirmed. As anticipated, the ability of the recommendation system to toss recommendations to and from inventors who are in various phases of their careers is confirmed. The capability of the system to toss intra-organizational as well as inter-organizational recommendations is also revealed. Attempt for recommendation system design for inventors with such an objective and verification of such an ability is done for the first time, at least according to our knowledge.
This investigation is not free from limitations and we see such limitations as opportunities for further research. As the system design is devoid of any supervised learning methods, the accuracy of the system cannot be assessed in the way it was done for the existing systems. Only post-implementation validation of accuracy is possible and this is reserved as a future exploration after the implementation of the system. The choice of an optimum threshold level for the selection of low priority recommendations has to be addressed for further improvement of the system. Another important factor to be considered in an operations perspective of the system is related to the optimization of recommendations i.e., the most suitable data organization of recommendations for the mailing/communicating system. The adjacency list representation could address this concern. These are some of the major possible future endeavors other than many (software) developmental aspects that are yet to be attended.
